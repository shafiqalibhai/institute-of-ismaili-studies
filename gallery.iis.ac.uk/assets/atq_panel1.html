Abstracts (in order of presentation)

Modern Shi‘i Authors on the Question of the Authenticity of the Text of the Qur’an (19th and 20th Centuries)
Rainer Brunner, Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique, Paris

The question of whether the established text of the Qur’an is authentic has always been a major issue for Shi‘i theologians, as it is claimed in numerous hadiths that the compilers of the text deliberately omitted many allusions and explicit references to the Ahl al-bayt as Prophet Muhammad’s legitimate successors.

In the course of the juristic evolution of Twelver Shi‘ism, after the disappearance of their twelfth imam, this allegation was relegated to the background, but the revival of Akhbari Shi‘ism between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries brought it back to the fore. After the final victory of the Usuli current, the issue seemed to be solved for good when the Iranian scholar Husayn Taqi al-Nuri al-Tabrisi (d. 1902) composed the Fasl al-khitab, a lengthy book in which he compiled all known references to the classical conviction of the falsification (tahrif) of the Qur’an.

Since then, the topic has figured prominently in (mostly polemical) debates between Sunnis and Shi‘is, and many modern Twelver Shi‘i writers deem it necessary to include explicit refutations of the classical Twelver doctrine in this regard. Most Qur’anic commentaries refer to it, and, more often than not, the question of tahrif proper is combined with other controversial theological issues, such as abrogation (naskh) or the changing will of God (bada).

A Text within a Text within a Text within a Text: The Bab’s Commentary on the Sura of Joseph
Todd Lawson, University of Toronto, Canada

If the literary activity of the virtuosi of the Islamic spiritual and mystical tradition is any guide to go by, then it could be said that a kind of World Wide Web has existed for quite a long time. This paper demonstrates how a self-styled tafsir on the twelfth sura of the Qur’an is in simultaneous conversation with numerous other sacred texts and commentaries. Treating significant words and clusters as ‘hot links’ we are able to plumb the commentarial gesture for the astonishing depth and mastery it reveals, and conceals.

The Qur’an and Contemporary Iranian Shi‘i Discourse on the Origins of Language
Aun Hasan Ali, McGill University, Canada

This paper deals with contemporary Twelver Shi‘i discourse on the origins of language. It explores how two contemporary Twelver Shi‘i scholars treat the question of the origins of language in different disciplines. The first scholar under consideration is Muhammad Baqir al-Sadr, whose main contribution to the tradition lies in the field of usul al-fiqh (the principles of jurisprudence). The second scholar is Mohammad Hosayn Tabataba’i, whose main contributions to the tradition lie in the fields of hikma (philosophy) and tafsir (Qur’anic exegesis).

Sadr treats the question of the origins of language in his textbook on usul al-fiqh entitled Durus fi ‘ilm al-usul, and Tabataba’i treats it in his voluminous exegesis of the Qur’an entitled al-Mizan fi tafsir al-Qur’an. The parameters of tafsir and, to some extent, even its content, are determined by ‘ilm usul al-fiqh. The paper concludes that the relationship between these two disciplines shows that ‘ilm usul al-fiqh is a foundational discipline and raises the spectre of nomocentrism.

However, as this paper’s presentation of Sadr’s discussion of the origin of language shows, simply because ‘ilm usul al-fiqh is seen to serve the law does not mean that it is purely about the law. With regard to tafsir, many contemporary Iranian Reformers, having embraced the modern category of ‘religion’, stress the centrality of a proper understanding of the Qur’an to a true understanding of Islam. They assume that the fact that the Qur’an is the most sacred Islamic text means that disciplines ancillary to it are foundational. The objective of tafsir is to understand the Qur’an, which partly explains the energy that contemporary Iranian Reformers have poured into its interpretation.

This paper, however, shows that tafsir is in reality a second-order endeavour, carried out in a discursive realm which ‘ilm usul al-fiqh inscribes.

Nusrat Amin (1886–1983) on Gender and Family Relations
Maryam Rutner, New York University

In a meta-textual analysis, this paper examines the interpretation of Qur’anic verses related to gender relations in the fifteen-volume tafsir entitled Makhzan al-‘irfan dar ‘ulum-i Qur’an by Nusrat Amin (1886–1983), a leading mujtahida in twentieth-century Iran.

The paper offers two major insights. First, it shows the mujtahida’s contribution to the larger body of exegetical works by comparing the interpretation of selected verses in Makhzan al-‘irfan to the interpretation of the same verses in other tafsirs. Second, the paper sheds light on Amin’s viewpoints about gender relations by drawing on three sets of literature.

On the one hand, relevant sections in Amin’s other scholarly and non-scholarly works will be analysed. Of special interest here is her non-scholarly book Rawish-i khushbakhti wa tawsiyya ba khaharan-i imani, which she dedicated to the general female readership. On the other hand, the paper will compare her viewpoints about gender relations to those of other contemporary scholars and ‘ulama’ during the Pahlavi era, especially to those of Murtada Mutahhari.

Lastly, the paper puts Amin’s arguments and Qur’anic interpretations on gender relations into the larger context of the literature that is concerned with female religious authorities and women/gender and the Qur’an.

The Qur’an and the Rethinking of Tradition in Post-Revolutionary Iran
Liyakat Takim, McMaster University, Canada

After the establishment of the Islamic Republic in Iran in 1979, many Twelver Shi‘i scholars started to examine the hermeneutical strategies of the Qur’an. This is because, for the first time, Shi‘i jurists had to respond to the practical needs of a modern state. Some of those scholars have argued that what is essential to a proper understanding of Islam is not the letter of the text but instead the spirit of the Qur’an and the Prophetic traditions. They maintain that there is no single valid interpretation of the Qur’an.

Jurists who argue for the reformulation of Islamic laws maintain that the interpretations of Islamic revelation were interwoven with the specificity of past circumstances. Jurists can only pronounce general principles, not rulings that are to be enforced at all times and places. They also argue that hermeneutical principles allow for a different understanding of the Islamic message. For the reform-minded jurists, it is essential that Muslims continue to review and revise the law in keeping with the dictates of their changing circumstances.

Scholars like Ayatollah Sana’i, Ayatollah Jannati, Ayatollah Mohaghegh Damad, Hujjat al-Islam, Muhsin Sa‘idzadeh and Mohsen Kadivar have called for a re-evaluation of traditional juridical pronouncements on many issues. As a matter of fact, discussions with some marja’s can reveal a distinct silent revolution within the seminaries in Qum.

This paper will examine the reinterpretation of Qur’anic laws in the seminaries in Iran. These include calls for reformulation on the hudud punishments (especially the punishment of stoning), women’s rights on the guardianship (wilaya) of minors and women’s rights to unilateral divorce.

In issuing juridical edicts, jurists have resorted to the ethical undertones of the Qur’an and shown a dissonance between the Qur’anic ethos and later juridical formulations that were premised primarily on hadith. The paper will also include observations on recent Qur’anic interpretations based on Liyakat Takim’s research undertaken in summer 2011.

Reading the Qur’an through Gender Lenses: A Contribution to a Gendered Ijtihad as Reflected in Iranian Women’s Journals
Anna Vanzan, Libera Università di Lingue e Comunicazione (IULM), Milan

Re-reading the Qur’an ‘as a woman’ is pivotal in the campaign by ‘Islamic feminists’ to defy the, so far, prevalent misogynistic and patriarchal interpretations of the holy text. In these last decades, much attention has been given to the revolutionary hermeneutic applied by Amina Wadud, an Afro-American convert who mainly operates in a non-Islamic society and who writes in English. However, insufficient attention has been paid to the reinterpreting methods applied by Iranian women in their context, i.e., in the frame of the Islamic Republic whose leadership claims to be the only repository of Qur’anic wisdom.

In Revolutionary Iran, some female theologians involved in a female-centred reading of the Qur’an have been sharing their endeavours with the lay public by publishing them in journals such as Payam-i Hajjari, Zanan, Pazhuhish-i zanan, Farzana and Nafa. Far from being elitist publications addressed to an exclusive academic/specialist milieu, these publications contributed to the widespread new awareness among Iranian women (and men) of the need for a nuwandish-i din, a new religious thinking capable of conciliating faith with human rights, pluralism and rationality.

By creating an open forum for discussing issues traditionally monopolised by the ‘ulama’, Iranian female periodicals not only favoured a gender-sensitive approach to the Qur’an, but also increased a general need and willingness to approach the holy text for a new and more responsible reading.

The first part of the paper considers the major issues of the ‘gendered-debate’ on Qur’anic verses as articulated in the major women’s magazines published in Iran. The second part updates the discussion on the topic by presenting opnions of Iranian female theologians, based on interviews conducted by Anna Vanzan over the last two years.

Biographies

Aun Hasan Ali is a PhD candidate at the Institute of Islamic Studies, McGill University, Canada. His primary research interest is the Twelver Shi‘i scholarly tradition. He has written several articles about Twelver Shi‘i jurisprudence, and is currently working on a dissertation about medieval developments in Twelver Shi‘ism.

Rainer Brunner was appointed Directeur de Recherche at the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS), Paris, in 2005. Before that, he was Assistant Professor at the Orientalisches Seminar of Freiburg University (1998–2004), Directeur d’Études Invité at the École Pratique des Hautes Études, Section Sciences Religieuses at the Sorbonne (2002), Fellow at the Institute for Advanced Studies of the Hebrew University of Jerusalem (2002/2003), and was a member of the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton (2004/2005). His main research interests are modern Muslim intellectual history, especially the history and theology of the Shi‘a, the relationship between Sunnis and Shi‘is, and Islam in Europe.

Todd Lawson is Associate Professor of Islamic Thought at the University of Toronto. He has published widely in Qur’anic studies including problems in classical tafsir, later Shi‘i tafsir and the development of scriptural commentary in both the Babi and the Baha’i religions. His two most recent books are The Crucifixon and the Qur’an: A Study in the History of Muslim Thought (Oxford, 2009) and Gnostic Apocalypse and Islam: Qur’an, tafsir, Messianism and the Literary Origins of the Babi Religion (Abingdon and New York, 2012). He is co-editor, with Sebastian Günther, of a two-volume collection on Islamic eschatology entitled ‘Roads to Paradise’ (in progress). He is currently working on the epic and apocalyptic literary dynamics of the Qur’an.

Maryam Rutner is a PhD candidate in Middle Eastern and Islamic Studies at New York University. Her interests include female religious authorities, religious education, and the relationship between the state and religion.

Liyakat Takim is the Sharjah Chair in Global Islam at McMaster University in Hamilton, Canada. A native of Zanzibar, Tanzania, he has spoken at more than eighty academic conferences and authored one hundred scholarly works on diverse topics such as reformation in the Islamic world, the treatment of women in Islamic law, Islam in America, the indigenisation of the Muslim community in America, dialogue in post-911 America, war and peace in the Islamic tradition, Islamic law, Islamic biographical literature, the charisma of the holy man and shrine culture, and Islamic mystical traditions. He teaches a wide range of courses on Islam and offers a course on comparative religions. Professor Takim’s publications include Shi‘ism in America (New York, 2009) and The Heirs of the Prophet: Charisma and Religious Authority in Shi‘ite Islam (Albany, NY, 2006). He is working on his third book ‘Ijtihad and Reformation in Islam’. Professor Takim has taught at several American and Canadian universities and is actively engaged in dialogue with different faith communities.

Anna Vanzan holds a PhD in Near Eastern Studies from New York University. She teaches Islamic and Arabic Culture at the ‘Statale’ University in Milan, and is Visiting Lecturer of Gender and Islamic Thought at the European Master in International Management (MIM) at Ca’ Foscari University in Venice. She has been working in gender studies with a particular focus on Asian Muslim societies. Her last book, Le donne di Allah, viaggio nei femminismi islamici (‘The Women of Allah, a Journey through Islamic Feminisms’, Milan, 2010) is dedicated to the emergence of Islamic feminisms, especially in Iran, Turkey, the Balkans, Malaysia and in the Western diaspora.
