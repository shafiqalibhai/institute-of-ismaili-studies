    Spain Trip Photo Gallery

    GPISH and STEP Field Trip to Historical Sites in Spain
    Wall Detail - Alhambra Palace Complex
    Detail of classical tiles that are synonymous with Alhambra along with Kufic Arabic inscriptions in stucco relief. The inscriptions are combined with geometrical patterns shaped into arabesque, decorated in coloured tiles and panelled on the wall. Each colour is an individual tile or alízares assembled like a mosaic.
    Looking out onto one of the many courtyard and gardens of the Alhambra palaces.
    Detail of wooden lattice window in the Alhambra.
    Detail of the stucco walls inside the Salón de los Embajadores or Hall of the ambassadors. It is the largest in the Alhambra and served as a grand reception room.
     
    The oldest surviving part of Alhambra is the Mexuar chambers where the administrative activities were carried out. Attached to the Mexuar is this oratorio overlooking El Albaicín, the room is orientated towards Mecca to the southeast.
     
    The intricacy, variation and sheer expanse of the stucco on the walls of the Alhambra has remained a constant source of wonder and inspiration through the centuries.
    The dome is a massive ribbed structure supported on flying arches. The interior of the dome is decorated with polychrome gold and glass mosaics organised symmetrically and also has inscriptions in Arabic.
    The mihrab of the Mosque of Cordoba was unprecedented for taking the form of an entire room rather than the traditional niche, and for being flanked by two rooms whose entrances are decorated with mosaics in a manner similar to that of the mihrab.
    Demonstrating breathtaking architecture within the Mezquita Córdoba, rows of marble pillars and colourful red and white arches give distinctive character to surroundings.
    Detail of red and white arches within the Mezquita Córdoba.
    The interior of Córdoba's Mezquita-Catedral, the prayer hall of the mosque.
     
    Faculty, Staff and Students outside the Palacio del Partal. El Partal is first palace to be built by the Nasrids in Alhambra and is smaller than their later palaces on the site. El Partal is famous for its reflective pool scene (see photo) and the amazing views of Albaicín. The rulers moved to the larger palaces after their construction.

    Alhambra (From Arabic al-Hamara; The Red): is a palace and fortress complex of the Moorish kings in Granada, built during the 13th and 14th centuries. The Alhambra's palaces were built for the last Muslim Emirs in Spain and its court, of the Nasrid dynasty. After the Reconquista (reconquest) by the Reyes Católicos ("Catholic Monarchs") in 1492, some portions were used by the Christian rulers. The Palace of Charles V, built by Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor in 1527, was inserted in the Alhambra within the Nasrid fortifications. After being allowed to fall into disrepair for centuries, the Alhambra was "discovered" in the 19th century by European scholars and travelers, with restorations commencing.

    Mezquita-Catedral de Córdoba: The Cathedral and former Great Mosque of Córdoba, in ecclesiastical terms the Catedral de Nuestra Señora de la Asunción (Cathedral of Our Lady of the Assumption), and known by the inhabitants of Córdoba as the Mezquita-Catedral.
