    Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani
    Dr Farhad Daftary

    This is an edited version of an article that was originally published in Encyclopaedia Iranica, Vol. XI, Columbia University, New York, 2003. pp. 639-641.



    Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani, Abu’l-Hasan Ahmad b. ‘Abd Allah b. Muhammad (d. after 411AH/ 1020-21CE), a prominent Isma‘ili da‘i and one of the most accomplished Isma‘ili theologians and philosophers of the Fatimid period. As in the case of other prominent da‘is, who observed strict secrecy in their activities while living in hostile milieus, few biographical details are available about him, although it is known that he flourished during the reign of the Fatimid Imam-Caliph al-Hakim bi Amr Allah (386-411 AH/ 996-1021CE). Al-Kirmani is not mentioned in any contemporary Muslim historical sources, but highlights of his life and career can be gathered from his own numerous extant works as well as the writings of the later Musta‘li Tayyibi Isma‘ili authors of Yemen.

    Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani’s date of birth remains unknown, but he was of Persian origin and his nisba suggests that he was born in the province of Kirman. Later in his life he maintained his contacts with the Isma‘ili community of that province and corresponded with subordinate da‘is there (see al-Kirmani’s al-Risala al-hawia and al-Risala al-kafia, in the Majmu‘at rasa’il al-Kirmani, pp. 102-12, 148-82; and Ivanow, 1942, pp. 142-45). He seems to have spent the greater part of his life as a Fatimid da‘i in Iraq, having been particularly active in Baghdad and Basra: the title of his lost al-Majalis al-Basriya wa’l-Baghdadiya (cited in his Kitab al-riad, p. 108) indicates that he delivered lectures (majalis) in these two cities.

    In Iraq, al-Kirmani successfully concentrated his efforts on local rulers and influential tribal chiefs. As a result, in 401 AH/ 1010-11 CE, Qirwash b. al-Muqallad, the ‘Uqaylid ruler of Kufa, Mawsil and several other towns, acknowledged the suzerainty of the Fatimid Imam-Caliph al-Hakim. In the same year, ‘Ali al-Asadi, chief of the Banu Asad, declared his loyalty to the Fatimids in Hilla and other districts under his control. Alarmed by the successes of the Fatimid da‘wa in Iraq, the ‘Abbasid Caliph al-Qadir took retaliatory measures. In 402 AH/1011 CE, he sponsored the so-called Baghdad manifesto to discredit the Fatimids, also refuting their ‘Alid ancestry (see Ibn al-Jawzi, VII, pp. 255-56; Ibn Taghribirdi, IV, pp. 229-31; Ibn Khaldun, I, pp. 45-46). The honorific title Hujjat al-‘Iraqayn, meaning the hujja or chief da‘i of both Iraqs (al-‘Iraq al-‘Arabi and al-‘Iraq al-‘Ajami), which is often added to al-Kirmani’s name and may be of a late origin, implies that he was also active in central and western parts of Persia.

    Al-Kirmani rose to prominence during the reign of Imam-Caliph al-Hakim, when the central headquarters of the Fatimid da‘wa in Cairo considered him as the most accomplished Isma‘ili theologian of the time. It was in that capacity that al-Kirmani played an important role in refuting the ideas of some dissident da‘is in Cairo, such as al-Akhram, Hamza and al-Darazi, who were then founding what was to become the Druze movement and religion. As part of the official Fatimid campaign against the dissident da‘is, who had also proclaimed Imam-Caliph al-Hakim’s divinity, al-Kirmani was summoned to Cairo in 405 AH/1014-15 CE, or shortly before then, where he produced several works in refutation of their doctrines. Having already elaborated the Fatimid understanding of the doctrine of the Imamate (see his al-Masabih fi ithbat al-imama), al-Kirmani now wrote a short treatise, the Mabasim al-bisharat, (included in Majmu‘at rasa’il, pp. 113-33; also ed. M. Kamil Husayn in Ta’ifat al-Duruz, pp. 55-74) dealing with the Imamate in general and al-Hakim’s Imamate in particular; he maintained that al-Hakim, like his predecessors, was only divinely-appointed, and not himself divine. In another treatise called al-Wa‘iza (Majmu‘at rasa’il, pp. 134-47; also edited by M. Kamil Husayn, pp. 1-29), composed in 408 AH/ 1017 CE as a reply to a pamphlet written by al-Akhram, al-Kirmani rejects the proclamation of Imam-Caliph al-Hakim’s divinity and accuses the dissenters of exaggeration (ghuluww) and infidelity (kufr). In these and other works, al-Kirmani also refuted the view of the Druze dissidents that the resurrection (qiyama) had already occurred with the appearance of Imam-Caliph al-Hakim, and that therefore the era of Islam had ended. Al-Kirmani argued that the era of Islam and the validity of its sacred law, the shari‘a, would continue under Imam-Caliph al-Hakim’s many Fatimid successors as Imams. His writings were widely circulated and to some extent succeeded in checking the spread of the doctrines associated with the initiation of the Druze movement. Subsequently, al-Kirmani returned to Iraq where, in 411 AH/1020-21 CE, he completed his last and major work, Rahat al-‘aql, shortly before his death.

    In addition to the Holy Qur’an and other Muslim sources, Hamid al-Din’s prolific scholarship was informed by the Hebrew text of the Old Testament, the Syriac version of the New Testament, and the post-Biblical Jewish writings (Kraus, 1931, pp. 243-46; repr. 1994, pp. 3-23). He expounded the Isma‘ili Shi‘i doctrine of the Imamate extensively. In a few treatises, particularly his Tanbih al-hadi wa’l-mustahdi, which remains unpublished, al-Kirmani refutes the theological views of the Zaydis, Ithna ‘asharis and other Muslim opponents of the Fatimid Isma‘ili Imams. In his al-aqwal al-dhahabiyya, he refutes the ideas of Abu Bakr al-Razi (d. 313 AH/ 925 CE) on the therapy of the mind which he had written in al-Tibb al-ruhani, defending the earlier criticisms of the (Carmathian; see CARMATHIANS) Isma‘ili da‘i Abu Hatim al-Razi (d. 322 AH/ 934 CE), who had argued for the necessity of revelation and prophethood while tracing all sciences to revelational origins (see al-Razi, 1977).

    Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani also belonged to that select group of Isma‘ili da‘is from Persia who amalgamated in an original manner their Isma‘ili theology (kalam) with philosophical traditions, in particular the type of Neoplatonism then current in the Muslim world. Al-Kirmani was fully acquainted with Aristotelian and Neoplatonic philosophies as well as the metaphysical systems of the Muslim philosophers (falasifa), notably al-Farabi and Avicenna, who was his contemporary. In his Kitab al-riad, al-Kirmani acted as an arbiter in a philosophical debate that had taken place earlier between some Iranian da‘is, including Muhammad al-Nasafi, Abu Ya‘qub al-Sijistani and Abu Hatim al-Razi (Ivanow, 1955, pp. 87-122). Al-Kirmani developed his own elaborate metaphysical system in his major philosophical treatise Rahat al-‘aql, which is written for adepts. In this book, al-Kirmani also propounded what may be regarded as the third stage in the development of Isma‘ili cosmology in medieval times. He replaced the Neoplatonic dyad of the Intellect (‘aql) and Soul (nafs) in the spiritual world, which had been adopted by his Iranian Isma‘ili predecessors, with a series of ten separate Intellects, in partial adaptation of al-Farabi’s Aristotelian cosmic system. However, al-Kirmani’s cosmology, which represents an original synthesis of different philosophical traditions, was not adopted by the Fatimid da‘wa; instead, it later provided the basis for the development of the fourth and final stage of Isma‘ili cosmology at the hands of Musta‘li-Tayyibi thinkers in Yemen.
