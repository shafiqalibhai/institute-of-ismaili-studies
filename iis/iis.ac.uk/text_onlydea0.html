    Faculty and Staff Bios
     

    (appearing in alphabetical order)

    Faculty

    Dr Alnoor Dhanani

    Alnoor Dhanani specializes in intellectual history, in particular the interaction between science, theology and philosophy in the civilisations of Islam. He has taught at Harvard University, Tufts University and The Institute of Ismaili Studies in London, where he was the Head of the Department of Graduate Studies. He is currently affiliated with Harvard University and Tufts University. His current research projects include reactions to Avicenna's critique of atomism, how atomists explain personal identity following resurrection and contemporary Muslim theological speculation on the Big Bang theory of the origin of the universe.

    Professor Zulfikar Hirji

    Zulfikar Hirji was born in Nairobi, Kenya and lived in Kampala, Uganda prior to immigrating to Canada in 1972. He is currently Associate Professor and Graduate Program Director in the Department of Anthropology at York University, Toronto. He received his DPhil from the University of Oxford (Social and Cultural Anthropology), MPhil from University of Cambridge (Islamic History), and B.A. (Joint Honours) from McGill University (Religious Studies and Anthropology). He has been a Research Associate at the Institute of Ismaili Studies and held a Post Doctoral Research Fellowship at Wolfson College (Oxford). He also studied classical Western music for more than a decade and performed semi-professionally as a vocalist.

    Prior to embarking on an academic career, he worked in the areas of communications, fundraising, marketing, education, skills training and enterprise for OXFAM Canada, the Canadian Catholic Organization for Development and Peace, the Government of Ontario and the Municipality of Metropolitan Toronto. He has also worked extensively as a volunteer in the Ismaili community in the areas of ultra-poverty, the elderly, youth, and arts and culture and has developed programmes, conducted workshops and moderated seminars for AKU in Central Asia and East Africa, AKDN in the UK, Ta’lim and STEP, FOCUS Canada, NCAB Canada, ITREB Canada, Council for Canada, and LIF projects.

    Professor Hirji has conducted archival research and ethnographic fieldwork in various parts of the world including South Asia, East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, Europe and North America. His academic research focuses on the social and cultural expressions of Muslims in historical and contemporary contexts. He is interested in how human societies articulate, represent and perform understandings of self, community and other. He is particularly concerned with the diverse ways in which Muslims express and articulate issues of deep human concern as well as matters of daily life. He also interrogates knowledge produced about Islam and Muslims by academics and others such as the media. His research interests have lead him to study a range of issues including the production of knowledge and the performance of identity, the role of cultural workers and social movements, the dynamics of family networks and inter-generational migration, the socio-legal formation of communal identity in colonial contexts, conflict in religiously plural societies, and contemporary interfaces between religion, art, science and technology.

    He supervises a range of Masters and Doctoral students and has taught extensively at the undergraduate and graduate levels. His courses include Anthropology of Islam and Muslim Societies, Ethnicity and Nationalism, Visual Anthropology, Anthropology of the Senses, Research Methods in Social Anthropology and the Arts of Islam and Muslim Societies. Professor Hirji has published a number of articles in edited volumes, journals and reference works. He is the co-author and co-editor of The Ismailis: An Illustrated History (Azimuth Editions & IIS, 2008), editor of Diversity and Pluralism in Muslim Contexts (I.B. Tauris & IIS, 2010), and editor of The Qur’an in Sub-Saharan Africa (OUP/IIS, forthcoming). His most recent book, Between Empires (Azimuth Editions, 2012), is an illustrated biography of Sheikh-Sir Mbarak al-Hinawy, the second Liwali (Governor) of the Coast of East Africa set within the context of colonial Mombasa. Launched in Muscat, Oman in 2012, the book has recently been translated and published in Swahili and Arabic.

    Shiraz Kabani

    Shiraz Kabani joined The Institute of Ismaili Studies in September 1999. After serving as Executive Officer (1999-2000), Head of Resource Development and Communications (2000-2005), and Head of Operations, Finance & Development (2005-2011), he now heads the Department of Community Relations.

    This department includes the portfolios of Community Education Programmes, International Training Programmes, STEP Deployment in the Field, Alumni Relations, Country Strategies to Enhance ITREBs, and HRD Programme for Central Asia. He also serves as acting head of the Department of Communications and Development, overseeing resource development, communications, and the IIS website.

    In addition to a BSc in Electrical Engineering and Computer Science and an MBA, Mr Kabani is currently pursuing a doctoral degree in Management, focussing on ethics and organisational leadership. His professional experience includes eleven years as Executive Officer of the Ismaili Tariqah and Religious Education Board for the USA and as the Managing Director of an IT consulting firm in Houston.

    Over the past 25 years, Mr Kabani has served as a public speaker and faculty member for human resource development programmes of the IIS and Ismaili community institutions in North America and Europe. He has also participated in the conceptualisation of various international projects for the Ismaili community. Currently, he is the Vice President of the Ismaili Council for the United Kingdom.

    Dr Mohamed Keshavjee

    Mohamed M. Keshavjee was called to the Bar at Gray's Inn, London, and Osgoode Hall Canada. He did his LLM at the University of London in Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR), Islamic Law, International Protection of Human Rights and Arab Comparative Commercial Law. He attained his doctorate in Law in ADR at the School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London, and has lectured to LLM students on ADR in diasporic Muslim communities at the London School of Economics.

    In 2010, he attended the Harvard Program on Negotiation under Frank Sander and in 2012 he trained family mediators in the European Union to conduct mediation in cases where The Hague Convention on International Child Abduction is inapplicable. Keshavjee is internationally recognised for his expertise on ADR among Muslims and has been invited as a keynote speaker to address several conferences on mediation. He has contributed chapters to influential books dealing with diasporic Muslim communities and legal pluralism in Europe.

    From 2000 to 2010 he coordinated, and lectured in, all the Ismaili Muslim community's training programmes on family and commercial mediation worldwide. He has also lectured on family mediation to students at the Muslim College in London. He is a member of the Board of Governors of The Institute of Ismaili Studies London anda member of the Advisory Committee of the International Social Services of Switzerland. He is also a member of the EU panel of International Family Mediators trained to deal with Hague Convention cases on international cross-border child abduction.

    Dr Farouk Mitha

    Farouk Mitha is a lecturer in the Faculty of Education at University of Victoria, Canada. He was Academic Course Director for the IIS Secondary Teacher Education Programme (STEP) from 2008-2011, and is currently involved with the IIS in developingcurricula for Human Resource Development Programmes. He has a B.A. in History from the University of Toronto, a M.A in Islamic Studies from McGill, a M.A in International Education from the University of London, and a Ph.D. in English Literature and Education from the University of Victoria. His current research is on the teaching of Shakespeare in secondary schools; and debates on the concept of culture in educational research.


    He has published in the area of medieval Islamic thought and on the state of liberal education in contemporary Muslim societies, as well as on Canadian literature and Iranian cinema.

    His book, Al-Ghazali and the Ismailis: A Debate on Reason and Authority in Medieval Islam was published by I.B Tauris.

    Professor Karen Ruffle

    Karen Ruffle, Assistant Professor of History of Religion and Women’s and Gender Studies (UTM) and Religion (UTSG), specialises in Indo-Persian Shiʿism. Her research and teaching interests focus on devotional texts, ritual practice, and Shiʿi material practices in South Asia. She has conducted field research in India, Pakistan, Iran, and Syria. Ruffle obtained her B.A. in Religion from Middlebury College and her M.A. and PhD in Religious Studies with a specialization in Islamic studies from the University of North Carolina-Chapel Hill. Her first book Gender, Sainthood, and Everyday Practice in South Asian Shi’ism was published by the University of North Carolina Press in 2011. Recent publications focus on Fatimah al-Zahra’s exceptional embodiment in Shiʿi hagiographical narratives; devotional literature and ritual practices in South Asia that centre on Fatimah; and Urdu didactic hagiographical narratives focusing on the special father-daughter relationship between the Prophet Muhammad and Fatimah. Ruffle’s most current publications focus on issues of Shiʿi material practices and the ritual performance of self-flagellation (matam). She is currently working on her second book, tentatively titled, Somatic Shiʿism: The Body in Deccani Shiʿi Material Religion and Ritual Practice.

    Dr Amyn B Sajoo

    Amyn Sajoo is Scholar-in-Residence at the Centre for Study of Muslim Societies & Cultures at Simon Fraser University in Vancouver, where he lectures in politics and the humanities — with a research focus on applied bio-social ethics. He was previously affiliated with Cambridge and McGill universities, as well as at the Institute of Ismaili Studies in London.

    Earlier, Amyn served as an advisor with the Canadian department of Justice in Ottawa — and as the 1993 Canada Fellow at the Institute of Southeast Asian Studies in Singapore, when he wrote his first book, Pluralism in Old Societies and New States. He was selected as the 2010 Visiting Academic in the Middle East by the Canadian Department of Foreign Affairs, which took him to Egypt, Syria and Jordan in the run up to the events of the “Arab Spring.” More recently, Amyn delivered the Distinguished Lecture in Islam at the University of Victoria, and the Augustana Lectures on Religion in Public Life at the University of Alberta.

    He is the author of Muslim Ethics: Emerging Vistas (2004), which includes a discussion of biomedical and political ethics, and contributing editor of Muslim Modernities (2008), and Civil Society in the Muslim World (2002). Amyn is the general editor of the IIS’s Muslim Heritage Series, which includes A Companion to Muslim Ethics (2010), and A Companion to Muslim Cultures (2012).

    Professor Walid Saleh

    Walid Saleh is professor of Islamic Studies at the University of Toronto. He is a specialist on the Qur'an, the history of its interpretation and Islamic intellectual history. He is the author of The Formation of the Classical Tafsir Tradition: A Qur’an Commentary of Al-Tha Labi (d. 427/1035) and In Defense of the Bible – A Critical Edition of an Introduction to Al-Biqis Bible Treatise. He is the author of numerous articles on various aspects of the history of the tafsir tradition.

     
    Staff/Volunteers

    Fatima Dhanani

    Fatima Dhanani gained a BSc in Anatomy and Cell Biology with Management from McGill University in 2004.She then went on to study for a LLB Bachelors of Law at the University of Leicester, which she completed in 2007.In 2008 she completed her Legal Practice Course (LPC) at BPP Law School London, before returning briefly to Canada.She recently completed her MA in Islamic Law from the School of Oriental and African Studies, University of London.

    She joined the Department of Community Relations at the IIS as Programmes Officer in May 2009 and went on to be the Education Officer. In 2011, she became the Research Officer in the Constituency Studies Unit. In 2013, Fatima rejoined the Department of Community Relations as the Education Coordinator and is responsible for the design and implementation of the intellectual content of education programmes currently undertaken by the department and the continued professionalisation of Jamati educators.
