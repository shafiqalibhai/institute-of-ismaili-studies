    A Journey into Education
    September 2012

    GPISH Alumni, Dr. Marodsilton Muborakshoeva, currently a STEP Lecturer, and Practicum Teaching and Learning Coordinator at The Institute of Ismaili Studies tells us about the journey that led her to GPISH and how she has continued to build on the foundation that GPISH gave her.

    My interest in teaching and research started while I was studying for my undergraduate degree at the Tajik State University. Upon completion, I was asked to remain at the faculty and embark upon post-graduate studies. I liked the idea but thought that a true scholar, especially in the teaching profession, should start from scratch and have experience of teaching at secondary schools first. However, my plan to return back to academia did not materialise immediately, because after one year of teaching I got married, had a family and, as they say, the rest is history.

    Somehow though, I did not give up my dream and kept in touch with the academic world through small opportunities which presented themselves from time to time. In 1995, a great opportunity came my way, and set me on the course that would bring me to the IIS and join GPISH. This was the year His Highness the Aga Khan visited Badakhshan. Immediately after his visit, teacher training courses were organised by the IIS for the implementation of the Ta‘lim programme in Badakhshan. As fortune would have it, the school where I was a teacher selected me to participate in these courses. In part, this was due to my enthusiasm for teaching culture and religion.

    My active participation in these teacher training courses, especially conducting and demonstrating ‘An exemplary class’ of teaching the Ta‘lim curriculum for over 100 course participants helped me to become familiar with community work and acquired my basic education about Islamic and, more specifically, Ismaili history. After going through the Khorog English Programme, I was selected to study at GPISH in 2000, which I successfully completed and was chosen as a valedictorian for my class. I am grateful to the teachers at GPISH who really opened my eyes by encouraging me to question everything and advising students not to take things at face value but remain true to Ismaili intellectual traditions where critical inquiry has always played a major part. While studying for my Masters and PhD degrees at Green-Templeton College, Oxford University, I realised how quality education transforms people and contributes to socio-cultural well-being of a society.

    Choosing a research topic at the graduate level, seems a daunting task at first, but once you carefully consider all your options and research interest, the charge becomes much easier. For me the choice fell on a topic which would connect my academic interest in education and Islamic studies. I then decided to explore the histories of higher educational institutions among Muslims with a view of exploring it at the PhD level where I would connect these past histories with current developments in higher education.

    Consequently, I developed the PhD thesis into a book, entitled Islam and Higher Education: Concepts, Challenges and Opportunities, published by Routledge this year. In the book I explore the concepts of a university in Islam by tracing the long-standing traditions of higher learning and how higher education evolved over the centuries. The historical and qualitative research enabled me to explore the topic in Muslim contexts first and then closely examine the concepts of a university in the country with a majority of Muslim population, the Islamic Republic of Pakistan.

    A major dilemma that caught my attention in the beginning was the degree of the gap between quality of education provided at universities in Muslim contexts and the world renowned universities such as Oxford, Cambridge, Sorbonne, Harvard and many others. What was so special about these universities that students from developing countries aspired to read there? What became of the once prominent centres like the House of Wisdom and the House of Knowledge and higher educational institutions such as Al-Azhar, Al-Qarawiyyin and Al-Zaytuna, some major features of which were utilised when Europeans established their universities in medieval ages? Critics, of course, will point to the seemingly obvious reasons of general historical developments in the Muslim world and Europe, especially the advent of modernity, colonialism, and neo-colonialism. Although valid points, these developments still do not shed real light on the ways universities are conceptualised or how they traverse the societal changes and define their roles in relation to their host society and ruling powers. If we take these aspects into account, then we should also ask whether or not the world-famous universities have always been at the forefront of knowledge creation. Have they always been at the core of economic development as it is often assumed?

    Exploring these and other related issues comparatively, my book further contends that there are many aspects in the lifespan of higher education institutions, similar to the life of individuals perhaps, which need to be explored at a deeper level so that one knows what a particular institution is all about. Doing this, will allow one to understand that the way a university is conceptualised often lies at the core of its relationship with the society at large. Universities worldwide, including those in Muslim contexts, have different experiences of finding their individual voices throughout the centuries. In this process universities in Muslim countries have been facing numerous challenges with which they are coping differently, again depending on their conceptualisation and socio-economic condition of the society in which they are located. Some examples of these challenges are the lack of academic freedom and pluralism, funding and resources, access and admission, quality of education, quality assurance, lack of a research culture, organisation of curriculum, linguistic issues, position of women in society, and challenges related to the issues of reconciliation of modernity with tradition. Nevertheless, my findings demonstrate that universities in Muslim contexts do have the potential of enhancing the quality of education and research. In this, some of the Pakistani higher education institutions such as the Aga Khan University, Lahore University of Management Sciences, National College of Arts, University of Management and Technology and many others have been able to overcome most of these challenges and are at the forefront of knowledge creation and providing quality education.

    As a lecturer on the Secondary Teacher Education Programme (STEP) at IIS, I feel privileged to be part of a programme that studies Muslim cultures and civilisations openly and critically and which does not face most of the challenges mentioned above that other higher education institutions encounter. This gives me confidence to embark on further research in higher education, including Ismailis’ contributions to higher learning through the centuries.
