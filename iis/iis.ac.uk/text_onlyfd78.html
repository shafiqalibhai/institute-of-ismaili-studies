    PAPER ABSTRACTS
    The Religious Inspiration of the Saqqakhana Artistic School in 1960s Iran: A Spiritual Pop Art?

    Alice Bombardier, Centre d’Analyse et d’Intervention Sociologiques–l’École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales (CADIS–EHESS)

    In the wake of the term saqqakhana (public fountain) coined by the art critic Karim Imami in 1962 to describe a particular art movement, Iranian painters and sculptors have incorporated objects of worship from Twelver Shi‘i ritual and elements of popular religiosity into their art works.

    Artist Parwiz Tanawwuli, for instance, reported that when he visited the tomb of Shah ʿAbd al-ʿAzim in the city of Rayy with another artist, Charles Hossein Zenderoudi, in the the late 1950s, he was fascinated by the religious posters representing old seals and ancient talismanic images that hung in the surrounding shops. The simplicity of form, the purity of colours and the combination of patterns that characterised those traditional objects inspired Tanawwuli to start a collection which has been repeatedly displayed in exhibitions over the years. Some of the paintings by Zenderoudi are also displayed along with Tanawwuli’s religious images.

    In this paper, I will explore the origins of this predominant artistic trend of twentieth-century Iran, which was inspired, at its inception, less by the Qur’an than by popular religious culture. Indeed, according to Karim Imami, during the Third Tehran Biennial in 1962, these art works did not convey the solemn and scholarly air of Iranian mosques, but rather the intimate and familiaratmosphere of the saqqakhana. The growing use of calligraphy by the artists of this school today, however, suggests an inspiration closer to the visual codes of the Qur’an. Thus, I will analyse the links forged by these artists with religious texts, monuments or traditional representations. I will also examine how apt the expression ‘Spiritual Pop Art’ – invented by Kamran Diba in Contemporary Art of the Islamic World (1982) – is in describing this artistic movement.

    Methods of Qur’an Instruction in Today’s Iran

    Mohammad Mesbahi, The Islamic College

    An examination of the primary and secondary institutions of Islamic education in Iran shows that educators have often overlooked the results of research in their effort to improve learning. However, Islamic education in most Iranian schools has changed and continues to do so in this respect. This paper will examine the five new methods of Qur’anic instruction introduced in the early 1970s and currently in use in Iranian schools, coupled with improvements made more recently, with the view of exploring their adoption in the teaching of the Qur’an.

    The five pedagogical methods of ‘repetition’, ‘construction’, ‘asking questions’, ‘use of narratives’ and ‘homogeneity’ will be presented with examples from the Qur’an. This paper will also propose that a more recent method that has been introduced in Iranian schools called content analysis is potentially one of the most important in the teaching of Qur’anic Studies.

    Qunut with Rumi: The Neighbourliness of Prayer and Poetry in Iran

    Niloofar Haeri, Johns Hopkins University, Baltimore

    This paper is based on fieldwork that began in Tehran in 2009 and which continues to be carried out among a group of educated middle-class women mostly in their sixties. Their religiosity offers a series of interesting insights that have rarely been noted in the literature on Islam and its practices. The paper will begin with an ethnographic overview of the Qur’an classes that the members of this group have been holding on a weekly basis for many years, along with weekly sessions on Jalal al-Din Rumi, and then move on to the women’s description and experiences of their performance of ritual prayer. It will address such questions as: What are the kinds of discussions that come up in the Qur’an classes? What are the ramifications of the fact that participants read from bilingual Qur’ans in Persian and Arabic?

    In the second section, the paper will offer an ethnographic account of the ritual prayer as performed by these women. Since almost all of them pray at home, and rarely go to the mosque, this will be a description of private prayer. Because of the deep familiarity of these women with the Qur’an, many of them are able to select suras based on their particular needs and thoughts on any given occasion. It was in search of the features of their private prayer that I came across the different choices of religious or poetic phrases recited during their invocation (qunut).

    The presentation ends with a discussion of the qunut as one of a number of sites where the neighbourliness of prayer and poetry is in evidence. It will examine the question of how poetry affects the reception of the Qur’an and the recitation of the ritual prayer and supplications (duʿa).

    Husayn Muhyi’l-Din Illahi Qumshaʾi’s In the Company of the Qur’an

    Leonard Lewisohn, University of Exeter

    In 2011, Iran’s leading mystical theologian Dr Husayn Muhyi’l-Din Illahi Qumshaʾi published a huge tome in Persian (935 pages long) entitled In the Company of the Qur’an: 365 Days with the Qur’an. This work of exegesis features selected verses from the Muslim scripture in the original Arabic, alongside Persian and English translations (Yusuf Ali) of the text, followed by several pages of commentary on the passages.

    Since its publication in a small print run of only 2,000 copies in 2011, in less than two years the book has undergone eleven editions and sold over 20,000 copies. To the masses of Iranian readers whose enthusiasm for In the Company of the Qur’an has turned it into a runaway bestseller, the work’s attraction results from a combination of factors. One factor is the author’s reputation, which precedes him. Qumshaʾi is a great orator and a household name in Iran, where he has spent a lifetime teaching Persian literature, Sufism and Qur’anic Studies. His frequent appearances on television and the eloquence of his public lectures often attract thousands of listeners. Another factor in the work’s appeal is that it uses the poetry of Rumi, Saʿdi, Shabistari, Hafiz and Jami to render original masterly commentaries on well-known beloved verses as well as lesser-known abstruse passages from the Qur’an.

    In my lecture I will translate and highlight select passages from In the Company of the Qur’an to show how and why a great thinker’s novel yet traditional engagement with Islam’s studia humanitatis and studia adabiyya has succeeded in making its ancient scripture attractive and comprehensible to a popular audience.

    Rational-Analytical Tafsir in Modern Iran: A Comparison between al-Mizan of ʿAllama Tabatabaʾi and the Tasnim of Ayatullah Jawadi Amuli

    Seyfeddin Kara, Durham University

    The aim of this paper is to undertake a comparative study of the rational-analytical methodology of two prominent Iranian scholars and exegetes – ʿAllama Tabatabaʾi (1903–1981) and Ayatullah Jawadi Amuli. Tabatabaʾi is known as one of the Twelver Shi‘i scholars who has most deeply influenced the intellectual and religious landscape of modern Iran. He is the author of the Tafsir al-Mizan, a twenty-volume commentary of the Qur’an written in Arabic between 1954 and 1972. The book is one of the most significant Shi‘i Qur’anic commentaries of the twentieth century, thanks to its unique approach to the interpretation of the Qur’an.

    Tabatabaʾi’s heavy reliance on the rational-analytical method differentiates al-Mizan from many of the other exegeses of the Qur’an. His method is based on the examination of a particular verse through reference to other relevant verses in order to unveil its inner meaning. His methodology also involves the use of his own opinion, where the exegete does not deem it to be in contradiction to other verses of the Qur’an and traditions.

    Jawadi Amuli is one of the most prominent students of Tabatabaʾi, whose private lectures he attended for twenty-five years. Ayatullah Amuli is currently one of the most respected Twelver Shi‘i scholars in Iran and has considerable influence in the religious colleges of the city of Qum. He is also an influential political figure, as he led the Friday prayers in Qum until his resignation in 2009. Over the past decade, Jawadi Amuli has lectured in a nationally famous tafsir lecture series. The lessons are delivered to seminary students in Qum and are compiled as a written commentary on the Qur’an, entitled the Tasnim. Similar to his teacher, Jawadi Amuli employs a rational-analytical methodology in the twenty-five volumes of Qur’an exegesis that have been published so far.

    Striving beyond the Balance (al-Mizan): The Exegetical Uses of the Qur’an in the Tabatabaʾi tariqa

    Sajjad Rizvi, University of Exeter

    While ʿAllama Tabatabaʾi’s major exegesis, al-Mizan, has been much lauded as perhaps the best comprehensive Shi‘i exegesis of the twentieth century, in which his skills as philosopher, theologian and exegete are clearly on display, a more careful analysis of the text raises doubts about such an assessment. In the introduction to al-Mizan, one finds a clear disavowal of both mystical and philosophical exegesis, and an assertion that in our attempts to make sense of the text we often impose our categories and desires upon the scripture.

    My paper will attempt to answer two questions. First: What constitutes Tabatabaʾi’s hermeneutics of the text in his non-exegetical works, and why does he wish to distance himself from philosophy whilst in other works insisting that the dictates of the faith are entirely compatible with, even identical to, the insights of philosophical theology (hikma ilahiyya)? Second: What does an analysis of the exegetical works of his disciples, such as Sayyid Muhammad Husayni Tihrani and others, tell us about modern attempts to evince mystical truths about the nature of reality? I hope to demonstrate that a careful textual examination allows us to offer an alternative reading of the discourse of mysticism and exegesis within the context of the Shi‘i seminary in Iran.

    Towards a New School of Qur’an Interpretation: The Maktab-i tafkik in Contemporary Iran

    Seyyed Mohammad Hadi Gerami, Imam Sadiq University, Tehran

    This paper provides a comprehensive account of Mirza Mahdi Isfahani’s (d. 1946) school of thought, which was characterised by an independent interpretive approach to the Qur’an, and it examines how such a school evolved. Mirza Mahdi Isfahani was the founder of the Shi‘i Maktab-i tafkik (the ‘School of Distinction), an independent theological school based in Mashhad.

    He believed that the Islamic creed ought to be independent from philosophy and mysticism, hence the word tafkik (‘distinction’) attached to his school of thought. In his reading of Islamic dogmatic systems, he emphasised that hadiths and Qur’anic verses were the most reliable primary sources for the justification of Islamic thought.

    The emergence of the Maktab-i tafkik has had a major impact on contemporary Twelver Shi‘i thinkers, particularly in the seminaries of Khorasan. After Isfahani’s death, a number of his disciples, including scholars such as Mirza Jawad Aqa Tihrani (d. 1989), Ayatullah Hasan Ali Murwarid (d. 1425/2004), Ayatullah Shaykh Mahmud Halabi (d. 1998) and Ayatullah Muhammad Baqir Maliki Miyanaji (d. 1419/1998), continued to propagate their master’s teachings.

    Ayatullah Maliki Miyanaji’s tafsir, Manahij al-Bayan, is the most significant commentary of the Qur’an to have been produced by the Maktab-i tafkik. This paper also investigates whether Maliki Miyanaji attempted to establish an independent school of interpretation based upon his mentor’s teachings.

    Should Husbands Discipline their Wives? Contemporary and Classical Iranian Interpretations of Q. 4:34

    Karen Bauer, The Institute of Ismaili Studies, London

    Q. 4:34 is one of the most controversial verses in the Qur’an today. It seems to speak of a hierarchical spousal relationship, in which a husband should pay his wife’s maintenance, a wife should obey her husband and a husband may discipline his wife if he fears her nushuz (recalcitrance).

    This paper outlines the content and method of contemporary Iranian approaches to Q. 4:34, and explores the relationship between contemporary and classical interpretations of the verse. I argue that no contemporary interpretation is an exact mirror of classical interpretations.

    Some contemporary interpreters agree with classical notions of a marital hierarchy, but the rationales behind the hierarchy have changed: they incorporate arguments that are said to be based in modern science. Other contemporary interpreters use modern scientific findings to argue against a marital hierarchy: they say that the verse was revealed in a specific set of historical circumstances that are not reflected in today’s world. Both groups draw on notions of common sense, fairness, justice and ethics.

    By exploring the boundaries of the ethical treatment of wives in both classical and contemporary Qur’an interpretation this paper sheds light on the mechanisms with which the Qur’an has been interpreted through time.

    Ayatullah Bujnurdi and the New Jurisprudence

    Liyakat Takim, McMaster University

    This paper will examine the reinterpretation of Qur’anic laws in the seminaries in Iran by looking at the thought of Ayatullah Bujnurdi.

    The establishment of an Islamic Republic in Iran in 1979 gave fresh impetus to the examination of the hermeneutical strategies employed to interpret the Qur’an. This was because, for the first time, Shi‘i jurists had to respond to the practical needs of a modern state.

    Some scholars argued that what was essential to a proper understanding of Islam was not the letter of the text but the spirit of the Qur’an and the Prophetic traditions. They maintained that there was no single valid interpretation of the Qur’an.

    Jurists who argue for the reformulation of Islamic laws maintain that the interpretations of Islamic revelation were interwoven with the specificities of those times and places; they believe that jurists can only pronounce general principles, not rulings that are to be enforced at all times and places. They also argue that hermeneutical principles allow for a different understanding of the Islamic message.

    For the reform-minded jurists, it is essential that Muslims continue to review and revise the law in keeping with the dictates of their changing circumstances. Since 1979, there has been a call for a radical rethinking of the religious tradition in Shi‘i circles. Such formulations have emanated from within the religious seminaries. Scholars such as Ayatullah Saniʿi, Ayatullah Jannati, Ayatullah Mohagheg Damad, Hujjatulislam Muhsin Saʿidzada and Mohsen Kadivar have called for a reevaluation of traditional juridical pronouncements on many issues.

    There is distinct evidence of a silent revolution within the seminaries in Qum. Reinterpretations include the reformulation of the hudud punishments (especially the punishment of stoning), women’s rights on guardianship (wilaya) of minors and women’s rights to unilateral divorce. In issuing juridical edicts, jurists have adhered to the ethical subtext of the Qur’an and shown the dissonance that exists between the Qur’anic ethos and the earlier juridical formulations that were premised primarily on traditions. The paper will also include observations on new Qur’anic interpretations based on my research work in the summer of 2011.

    Qur’anic Sanction and Sovereignty in Contemporary Iran

    Neguin Yavari, The New School for Social Research

    This paper will compare elaborations by some contemporary intellectuals on amr bi’l-maʿruf (enjoining the good) with the views of Ayatullah Khomeini and other Muslim intellectuals on the same subject in order to study the role of Qur’anic sanction in Iran’s contemporary political lexicon.

    In 2009, after the reformist faction lost the presidential elections, important theoreticians of the reformist camp have elaborated on the concept of Amr bi’l-maʿruf wa nahy ʿan al-munkar. These works called for this salient command from traditionalist Muslim dogma to be revived in Iranian politics, upholding the view that the application of ethics and morality in governance was the solution to Iran’s political woes. This espousal of amr bi’l-maʿruf reflected an effort by the reformist camp to reclaim the mantle of traditional Islam – all in an attempt to make palatable their progressive political agenda. In that respect, they followed in the footsteps of Khomeini himself, who succeeded in crafting innovative and revolutionary doctrines – prime among them wilayat-i faqih – in the garb of tradition and custom.

    The Qur’an has emerged in the twenty-first century as the arbiter par excellence of the practice of politics in the Islamic world. As Reinhard Schulze has posited, Islamic political discourse of the late twentieth century has shed its utopian views and shuns Prophetic tradition (sunna) in favour of the hermeneutics of the Qur’an. The very secularisation of the sacral is in evidence when reformist intellectuals turn to the Qur’an to resolve contemporary political issues. As it is considered to be the constitution of the sovereign Islamic state, the Qur’an functions as lex scripta, and thus works to supplant the shariʿa itself. This new written law was engendered, interestingly, only after the very guardians of that shariʿa, the ʿulamaʾ.assumed authority.

    The Soroushian Theory of Revelation: An Epistemological Assessment

    Yaser Mirdamadi, University of Edinburgh

    Although the divine character of the words and meaning of the Qur’an is currently held to be orthodox doctrine by the vast majority of Muslim scholars, it was not so during the first three centuries of Islam. Recently, some modern Muslim intellectuals have called this doctrine into question. Influenced by Muslim philosophy and mysticism as well as modern epistemology and the philosophy of science, the Iranian intellectual and philosopher Abdolkarim Soroush (b. 1945) has argued that the Qur’anic revelation was the result of a religious experience undergone by Muhammad. He therefore believes that the revelation is a human and accidental (ʿaradi) product rather than a divine and essential (dhati) one. Qur’anic revelation, according to him, is divine only in the sense that it is an outcome of the human encounter with the divine.

    This paper will first elucidate Soroush’s theory of revelation and show that his conception of revelation is similar to that of a minority of Muʿtazilis. It will then look at the implications of the theory for the relationship between religion and science as well as for prophetology. Soroush uses the approach of critical realism to arrive at his theory of revelation, and the paper will show that this method is problematic for the interpretation of the Muslim revelation. However, the paper will also argue that Soroush’s theory has been successful in broadening the thinking and improving the tolerance of jurists in contemporary Iran.

    From the Human Reading of Religion to the Prophetic Reading of the World: Muhammad Mujtahid Shabistari’s Hermeneutic Turn

    Mirjam Künkler, Princeton University

    Muhammad Mujtahid Shabistari (b. 1936) has publicly called for the acceptance of critical hermeneutic methodologies in the study of the Qur’an and Sunna and has started to re-read and analyse the Qur’an with tools developed in linguistic philosophy. The application of such an approach to the study of the religious sources, their evolution and redaction, Shabistari suggests, would not harm the appeal of revelation but, on the contrary, strengthen religious belief.

    This paper examines the secular hermeneutic Shabistari developed in his 2006 text Qiraʾat-i Nabawiaz Jahan (‘The Prophetic Reading of the World’) and the implications of this reading of the Qur’an for the nature of social order and political power. It also discusses the radical re-orientation Shabistari has undertaken with this view compared to earlier epistemological views expressed in Naqdi bar Qiraʾat-i Rasmi az Din (‘A Critique of the Official Reading of Religion’) and Taʿammulati dar Qiraʾat-i Insan az Din (‘Reflections on the Human Reading of Religion’).

    The Qur’an and the Political Discourse of the ʿUlamaʾ During the Constitutional Movement in Iran

    Denis Hermann, Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS)

    On 5 October 1906, the first parliament of the Muslim World was established in Iran, a few years before the reinstatement of constitutional and parliamentary rule in Istanbul in July 1908. The constitutional movement, which marked a turning point in the history of Iran, was a multi-ideological movement, where a broad range of people with varying ideas and ideals made public their vision for a new Iran through an array of publications. Among the ranks of these different actors, Twelver Shi‘i clerics were certainly among the most active and influential. The ʿulamaʾ produced an important body of politico-theological literature in which they either supported or condemned the idea of a constitutionalist Iran. Within this literature, the Qur’an and other religious scriptural sources such as hadith played a central role.

    This paper will first examine how the pro- and anti-constitutionalists made general references to the Qu’ran in their treatises. Next, it will discuss the arguments of two specific clerics whose works drew extensively on the Qu’ran and Hadith – Shaykh Husayn Ahrumi Bushihri (d. 1922–3), the author of the Ihyaʾ al-milla, and Shaykh Abu’l-Hasan Najafi Marandi, author of the Dalaʾil bar barahin al-Furqan fī butlan-i qawanin-i nawasikh-i muhkamat-i al-Qurʾan.

    Modern Shi‘i Authors on the Question of the Authenticity of the Text of the Qur’an (Nineteenth and Twentieth Centuries)

    Rainer Brunner, Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS)

    Whether or not the established text of the Qur’an is genuine has always been a major question for Twelver Shi‘i theologians, as it is claimed in numerous Shi‘i hadiths that the compilers of the text deliberately omitted many allusions and explicit references to the Ahl al-bayt as Muhammad’s legitimate successors.

    In the course of the juristic evolution of Twelver Shi‘ism, after the disappearance of the twelfth imam, this allegation was relegated to the background; however, the revival of Akhbari Shi‘ism between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries brought it back to the forefront. After the final victory of the rival Usuli current, the issue seemed to be solved for good when the Iranian scholar Husayn Taqi al-Nuri al-Tabrisi (d. 1902) compiled all known references to the classical conviction of the falsification (tahrif) of the Qur’an. Since then, the topic has figured prominently in (mostly polemical) debates between the Sunnis and Shi‘is, and many modern Twelver Shi‘i writers deem it necessary to provide explicit refutations of the early Shi‘i doctrine in this regard. Most Qur’anic commentaries refer to it and, more often than not, the question of tahrif proper is combined with other controversial theological issues, such as abrogation (naskh) or the ‘changing will’ of God (badaʿ).

    ‘And they thought, as you thought, that God will by no means send anyone again’ (Q. 72:7): A Re-Conception of the Islamic Doctrine of Finality

    Omid Ghaemmaghami, American University in Cairo

    The doctrine of the finality of Prophet Muhammad’s prophethood is one of the most fundamental tenets held by Muslims. Although recent scholarship has shown convincingly that this doctrine did not enjoy universal acceptance in early Islamic history, most exegetes of the Qur’an have contended, based on their reading of Q. 33:40, that divine revelation was completed by Prophet Muhammad, God’s final Prophet and Messenger. This status of Prophet Muhammad has often been exploited to undermine – if not openly condemn – post-Islamic claims to revelation. The most salient of such claims in the modern period was advanced by the nineteenth-century Iranian thinker Mirza Husayn Nuri, known as Bahaʾullah (d. 1892), the founder of the Bahaʾi religion.

    This presentation will analyse hitherto neglected works of Bahaʾullah, such as his Surat al-Sabr, which offer not only a new exegesis of several Qur’anic verses but, more importantly, attempt to conceive a dialectic hermeneutic that fuses prophecy, revelation and resurrection. It was partly on this hermeneutic foundation that Bahaʾullah based his claim of post-Islamic revelation.

    I will begin by briefly examining the doctrine of finality as explicated by six prominent classical and contemporary Persian Qur’an commentators so that we may appreciate how Bahaʾullah’s break from this tradition was not only unique in the annals of Iranian Islam but also more challenging, intricate and multifaceted than what has thus far been understood.

    Speaking the Secrets of Sanctity in the Tafsir of Safi ʿAli Shah

    Nicholas Boylston, Georgetown University

    The Tafsir-i Safi is the first and most important known translation and commentary on the entire Qur’an in Persian rhyming couplets. It was written by the Niʿmatullahi master Safi ʿAli Shah (d. 1315/1898). However, despite its great significance, this work has not yet received the scholarly attention or recognition that it deserves.

    This paper will examine the Tafsir-i Safi within the social and historical context in which it was written: Qajar Iran and Niʿmatullahi Sufism. It will consider the unique characteristics of the translation and particularly the tafsir, and will cover such topics as the literary aspects of the work, the characteristics that indicate that the text is a unique example of Sufi tafsir, and the identification of the text as a work of Shi‘i Sufism. In addition to highlighting the importance of the metaphysics of the school of Ibn ʿArabi within the work, the paper will also show the importance of Safi ʿAli Shah’s Twelver Shi‘i identity and the way in which he negotiates a flexible yet assertive engagement with the Sunni tradition.

    The paper ends with a consideration of those passages in which Safi ʿAli Shah interrupts the tafsir, having been swept up by the mystical ecstasy. I suggest that these ecstatic passages, which reveal Safi ʿAli Shah’s views of his own work and indicate the states of consciousness in which he wrote it, provide the material for what may be termed a phenomenology of mystical exegesis.

    Sufism, Exegesis and Society in Early Modern Iran: Sultan ʿAli Shah’s Bayan al-saʿada and the Status of Sufi Qur’anic Exegesis

    Alessandro Cancian, The Institute of Ismaili Studies

    This paper is a presentation of the main exegetical work, the tafsir Bayan al-saʿada fi maqamat al-ʿibada, of Sultan ʿAli Shah Gunabadi (d. 1909), the eponymous master of the Gunabadi branch of the Niʿmatullahi Sufi order.

    Sultan ʿAli Shah was a prolific writer whose exegetical activity was not limited to his tafsir. His hermeneutical principles are reflected throughout his literary productions, particularly in his Saʿadat-nama, as well as his Majmaʿ al-saʿadat and his fascinating commentary on the aphorisms of the mystical poet Baba Tahir, the Tawdih.

    The exegetical activity of Sultan ʿAli Shah influenced the successive intellectual output of the order and had an impact on mystical exegesis even outside of it.

    The Bayan, which combines features of the pre-Buwayhid, Hadith-based commentaries with those of the classical mystical tafsirs, is the master’s most important, influential and authoritative statement. Written in Arabic and circulated at a time when Iran was involved in the painful entrance into modernity that culminated in the Constitutional Revolution of 1906, the Bayan was really a compendium of its author’s doctrines, clad in the form of a Qur’anic commentary. Through its style and content, the Bayan builds a hermeneutic bridge between classical and modern Twelver Shi‘i exegesis. Its style and content also represent the means through which the order sought to gain recognition among the mainstream ʿulama. This process was initiated by the second generation of Niʿmatullahi masters after the order’s return to Persia in the late eighteenth century. However, they were never able to fully overcome this centuries-old conflict over the religious legitimacy of Sufism.

    Defending the Qur’an in Qajar Iran: Husayn ʿAli Shah and his Refutation of Henry Martyn

    Reza Tabandeh, University of Exeter

    This paper presents the views on the Qur’an of Ḥusayn ʿAli Shah (d. 1818), the first Iranian to become master of the Niʿmatullahi order after the order’s return to its homeland from India. Ḥusayn ʿAli Shah, who was a Sufi as well as a scholar, refrained from overtly propagating his doctrines out of caution, as he lived at a time when Sufis were persecuted for their beliefs. Hence, the only text written by him is a treatise refuting the work of the Protestant missionary Henry Martyn, who went to Persia to try to prove that the Qur’an was not a divine book but a man-made one. Husayn ʿAli Shah’s risala, entitled Radd-i padri, is of considerable importance as it was the first text written in refutation of Martyn and was, in a way, taken as a reference for later responses (jawabiyya). This kind of writing became a genre of Shi‘i religious writing in its own right during the Qajar era.

    Husayn ʿAli Shah’s treatise takes the doctrine of the inseparability of all religious revelations to be a basic and irrefutable tenet. Consistent with a widely held Muslim approach, the treatise also speaks of the superiority of the Qur’an over other miracles among Abrahamic prophets. The originality of his approach, however, lies in his mystical approach to the matter, whereby he uses symbols and metaphors to refer to the sacred Book, in keeping with the Sufi tradition. In his discourse, Husayn ʿAli Shah combines Sufi and Shi‘i hermeneutics, posing the centrality of the wilaya of ʿAli as a cornerstone in the mystical understanding of the Qur’an.

    This treatise has not been properly studied so far. The analysis of it here will introduce the academic community to Husayn ʿAli Shah’s view on the Qur’an and its interpretation, and thus contribute to the advancement of the knowledge of Shi‘i Sufi approaches to the Qur’an.

    The Divine Word on the Screen: Religious Epics in Iranian Cinema

    Nacim Pak-Shiraz, University of Edinburgh

    The vast financial State support available for the production of religious films has led to the recent emergence of a cinematic genre in Iran that can loosely be referred to as religious epics. Whilst the genre has had a long history in Western cinema, it is a fairly recent occurrence within Iranian cinema.

    This paper aims to examine the emergence of religious epics within Iranian cinema and will specifically focus on the Qur’anic epic. Whilst much has been written on the Biblical epic within Western cinema, and particularly Hollywood cinema, no academic attention has been paid to films which derive their narratives from the Qur’an. Indeed, even the term Qur’anic epic does not exist as a conceptual category. Studies of the Qur’an so far have mainly focused on its textual analysis strictly within the theological and jurisprudential fields. However, none have looked at how cinema has been employed as a new medium for engaging with the Qur’an. This paper aims to initiate this debate within both film studies and Islamic studies.

    Within the Western context, the production of Biblical films almost immediately followed the birth of cinema. In Iranian cinema, however, the epic genre is a fairly late development. The new millennium has witnessed the production of several big budget films that derive their stories from the Qur’an, such as Shahriyar Bahrani’s Saint Mary (2002) and Kingdom of Solomon (2010), and Muhammad Rida Warzi’s Abraham the Friend of God (2005). This paper will use Kingdom of Solomon as a case study to examine the development of the Qur’anic epic genre. Even though the Kingdom of Solomon narrates the story of a pre-Islamic prophet, embedded within it is a Shi‘i interpretation of the Qur’an, it retains a sense of universality and thus attempts to appeal to Muslims in the Muslim world and to western viewers as well.

    The Qur’an and Female Calligraphers in Iran

    Anna Vanzan, University of Milan

    ‘. . . and think that when I tried to elaborate upon calligraphy as a young painter in 1950s Iran I was told that khattniwis was a virus to kill!’

    These words belong to Mansura Husayni (1926–2012), one of the first Iranian artists to make extensive use of calligraphy in abstract painting from as early as the 1950s. In Iran (as well as in many other Islamic countries), calligraphy is enjoying a renaissance today. Many modern artists adopt traditional techniques mainly as a point of reference from which to embark on new explorations, and the sources of inspiration for the majority of them continue to be both Persian poetry and the Qur’an.

    As is common in many other artistic fields, in calligraphy there is an increasing female presence; at the moment there are at least 1,500 female calligraphers in the Islamic Republic of Iran. Some of them are exclusively involved in elaborating prayers and passages from the Qur’an and have gained public recognition in exhibitions sponsored by the government; others rely on a more variegated production. What, though, is the reason for the growing numbers of women entering the field of calligraphy which, in the past, was dominated by men? Is it simply their wish to spread a spiritual message? Is it due to their growing desire to be more visible in religious activities? Do female calligraphers believe that this presence will grant them more religious authority? This paper tries to answer these questions, partly relying on interviews conducted with Iranian female calligraphers.

    ‘I leave behind me two great things, the Book of God and my Family’: Comprehending the Qur’an within the Realm of the Ahl al-Bayt in Iranian Shi‘i Lived Religion

    Ingvild Flaskerud, University of Oslo

    In this paper I will discuss how a contemporary network of Shi‘i women in Shiraz engage with the Qur’an, and I will show how such engagement reflects Twelver Shi‘ism as a ‘lived religion’. By lived religion, I here refer to how the sacred text, the Qur’an, is treated as a living text, in the sense that it is perceived as being able to protect and guide people in their everyday lives and to connect everyday life to a much broader perspective on life which encompasses the eschatological vision of Twelver Shi‘ism.

    To this end, I discuss a celebration of Laylat al-Qadr and a number of Qur’an classes in which the women of this network participate. The performance of Laylat al-Qadr on 21 Ramadan is framed by two other liturgical settings, the fast and the mourning assembly commemorating imam ʿAli’s death. Oral tafsir and debates raised during Qur’an classes similarly refer to the lives of the Ahl al-Bayt in an attempt to apply the sacred word of God as guidance in everyday life. The ethnographic data thus offers examples of how the Qur’an in a Shi‘i environment is being associated with the Ahl al-Bayt in order to serve as a guiding principle to the way of life of Iranian Muslims.

    The Qur’an as an Aesthetic Model in Music: The Case of Twentieth-Century Iran

    Giovanni De Zorzi, Ca’ Foscari University of Venice

    In the tradition of Islam, the cantillation (qiraʾa) of the Qur’an is not considered to be a form of music (musiqi) or singing (sawt, ghina) and it cannot be adapted to musical modes and rhythms, as was clearly stated by al-Ghazali (d. 506/1111). From a more global view, this problematic status is a common feature of various religions dealing with the recitation of a sacred text.

    This has led ethnomusicologists to coin the term ‘cantillation’ in order to define a practice which lies somewhere between singing and recitation. Particularly in Persian music, the voice has a special role and it lies at the centre of the musical performance, regardless of the genre. The way the Qur’an is recited is closely linked with poetry and versification (ʿarud). The Qur’an is not poetry, but the rhymed nature of its sajʿ and the duration values of the text influence its vocal performance. In the cantillation of the Qur’an, the use of pauses, silences and modulations, which are so pervasive in Iran and elsewhere, tends to create an aesthetic model that has influenced musicians and their performances. Such an influence is clearly detectable in the unmetred forms, both vocal (awaz) and instrumental (taqsim).

    Evidence of this influence is visible in classical music (dastgahi, radifi), as a number of great singers often had an upbringing in, and thus drew upon their experiences with, the cantillation of the Qur’an. In Persian culture, we should remember the examples of Iqbal Sultan Azar (1866–1971), Ghulam-Husayn Banan (1911–1986) and Muhammad-Rida Shajariyan (b. 1940); the latter is widely acclaimed as Iran’s greatest living classical singer and musician (radif). In this paper, I intend to show the connections between the cantillation of the Qur’an and twentieth-century Iranian classical singing.
