Knowledge and Liberation: A Treatise on Philosophical Theology
Dr Faquir M Hunzai


Ed. and tr., F. M. Hunzai, London: I.B. Tauris in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 1998 (paperback with corrections 1999), pp. xii + 132 (English) + 92 (Persian).

ISBN (Hardback): 1 86064 217 9
ISBN (Softback): 1 86064 471 6

Synopsis

Publication page on Google Books

Download PDF version of Introduction (224 KB)

As the first English translation in over 50 years of any of Nasir Khusraw’s philosophical or theological texts, this critical edition and translation by Dr. Hunzai represents a landmark in Ismaili scholarship. Nasir Khusraw (1004- ca.1077), who is ranked among the highest poets in the Persian language and famed also for his detailed and frank travelogue written about his seven-year journey from Central Asia to Jerusalem, Cairo, Mecca and home again, was a leading Ismaili advocate (da‘i). In that capacity, as preacher and teacher, he composed a good number of religious and philosophical texts in Persian. During the 20th century, six of these works were edited from manuscripts and subsequently published, but only one was translated into English (W. Ivanow’s 1949 translation of the prose Rawshana’i-nama, also known as Six Chapters); an Italian translation of the current work, Gushayish wa Rahayish was published in 1959 by P. Filippani-Ronconi; and in 1990, the Jami‘ al-Hikmatayn came out in French translation by I. de Gastines.

This book is an important tool for scholars and students since Dr. Hunzai presents both the original Persian (in a new critical edition) as well as the accessible translation into English. In addition, the volume benefits from a brief but helpful introduction and a commentary to the text, both written by Professor Parviz Morewedge. These two essays indeed ‘bracket’ the text and provide a historical and intellectual context for understanding the philosophical thought of Nasir Khusraw.

The 22-page Introduction shows the philosophical threads , such as Plato and Aristotle, but particularly Plotinus (d. 270), which influenced Nasir and other Muslim thinkers, in that “the majority of Muslim philosophers sought to accommodate the emanational doctrine of Plotinus to the creation out-of-nothing form of Biblical and Qur’anic cosmogony”. In addition to an overview of Nasir’s life and works, the Introduction discusses two of his specific doctrines: how existence cannot be attributed to God and the meaning of ‘present time’. In the subsection, ‘Theodicy, Ethics and Salvation’, Nasir’s treatment of the necessity of the body for the salvation of the soul is shown, along with his defence of eternal reward or punishment for human actions based on the fact that humans have an intellect and shame and can use knowledge to determine their actions.

The English translation of the Gushayish wa Rahayish covers 91 pages (pp. 23-114), in which Nasir Khusraw responds to thirty questions on a variety of religious topics. Questions 1-5 deal with issues of cosmogony, such as what the Creator was doing before creation and how the eternal generated a creation in time. In Nasir’s unique delineation of time, the act of creation is conceived as instantaneous, beyond temporal priority and posteriority. Thus, the difference between the eternal and the created is treated as no more than a metaphor, and thereby resolved as a problem. Questions 6-11 cover topics dealing with existence and being, and the mind-soul debate. Questions 12-20 treat the contrast between form and matter, the physical and spiritual worlds, and physics of certain material phenomena, such as water, minerals, plants and animals. Questions 21-25 centre on philosophical theology, addressing topics such as the Divine Word, the createdness or eternity of the Qur’an, and the unity of God. The final section, questions 26-30, considers issues of theodicy, human salvation through knowledge, the meaning of judgment, the straight path, and the scales (all in an interiorised sense), and the difference between free-will and determinism.

The 10-page Analytical Commentary provides brief explanations of Nasir Khusraw's arguments in each chapter, and makes comparisons with Greek, Muslim, and European thinkers such as Pythagoras (and the others already mentioned), Ibn Sina, Nasir al-Din Tusi, Heidegger, Descartes, and Kant.

Remarkable for his tremendous influence in spreading the teachings of the Ismaili faith, as well as for his erudition and skill in Persian poetry and prose, Nasir Khusraw stands as a major figure in Ismaili and Islamic thought. As the first of his philosophical texts to receive critical treatment in English, this volume is a powerful entry into the growing field of Nasir Khusraw studies.
