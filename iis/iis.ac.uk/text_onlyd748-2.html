    The Qur'an and Adab: Biographies

    The Qur’an And Adab Traditions
    The Institute of Ismaili Studies, London
    24 – 26 April 2012

    Conference description
    Conference programme

    Eisa Ali Alakoub holds a PhD in Criticism and Rhetoric from Damascus University. He teaches at Aleppo University. He has published a number of books including The Influence of Persian Proverbs on Arabic Literature, Emotion and the poetic Genius (A critical study) and Arab Critical Thinking. He also translated a number of workd of Toshihiko Izutsu into Arabic.

    Top of Page


    Omar Ali-de-Unzaga is a Research Associate in the Department of Academic Research and Publications and Academic Coordinator of the Qur’anic Studies Research Unit of The Institute of Ismaili Studies. He completed his PhD at the University of Cambridge, on the use of the Qur’an in the Epistles of the Pure Brethren (Ikhwan al-Safa’). He is currently preparing a publication under the working title: A Philosophical Reading of Scripture: The Qur’an in the Epistles of the Pure Brethren, as well as an English translation and a critical edition of the Epistle on Character Traits (al-Risala fi’l-akhlaq) of the Ikhwan al-Safa’.

    Top of Page


    Nuha al-Sha‘ar is a Research Associate in the Qur’anic Studies Unit, Department of Research and Publications at The Institute of Ismaili Studies. She completed her PhD on Islamic ethics and political thought at the University of Cambridge. Her thesis title is “Abu Hayyan al-Tawhidi and Ethical Thought in the Fourth Century of Islam,” which she is currently revising for publication. With Verena Klemm, she co-edited “Sources and Approaches Across Disciplines in Near Eastern Studies: Proceedings of the 24th Congress of L'Union Européenne des Arabisants et Islamisants (Peeters, Leuven, 2012).

    Top of Page


    Sarah bin Tyeer holds an MA in English & Comparative Literature from the American University in Cairo and a PhD in Near and Middle Eastern Studies from SOAS with a specialisation in Qur’anic Studies and premodern Arabic literature. Her research interests revolve around the Qur’an, as well as the influence of the Qur’an on Arabic literature. She is currently a Research Associate and a Senior Teaching Fellow in Arabic at the School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS).

    Top of Page


    Ghassan El Masri studied Philosophy at the American University of Beirut and the University of Calgary, and holds a PhD in Arabic Studies from Freie Universität Berlin. In his research, instead of treating the Qur’an as a holy text sui generis, Dr. Masri analyses its literary features, using modern philological methods. His aim is to place the Qur’an in its context, and particularly to set it in relation to the Arabic poetry and literature of its time. Dr. Masri intends to open up the literary meanings of the Qur'an to the modern reader, both Muslim and non-Muslim alike.

    Top of Page


    Beatrice Gruendler (DEUG Strasbourg, 1985; BA Tübingen, 1987; MA 1989, PhD Harvard, 1995), Professor at Yale University, Fellow 2010-11, is active in four areas of research: the development of Arabic script, classical Arabic poetry and its social context, the integration of modern literary theory into the study of Near Eastern literatures, and early Arabic book-culture viewed within the history of media. Her publications include The Development of the Arabic Scripts: From the Nabatean Era to the First Islamic Century (Atlanta: Scholars Press, 1993), Medieval Arabic Praise Poetry: Ibn al-Rumi and the Patron's Redemption (London: Routledge Curzon, 2003), and, as contributing editor, Classical Arabic Humanities in Their Own Terms (Leiden: Brill, 2007). Her current work comprises a study of the communicative choices of literati in the ninth century CE and a media history of early Arabic book culture.

    Top of Page


    Louise Marlow teaches at Wellesley College. She received her undergraduate degree from Cambridge University and her PhD from Princeton University. She studies Arabic and Persian literature of the 9th-13th centuries CE and is particularly interested in mirrors for princes.

    Top of Page


    Bilal Orfali is Assistant Professor of Arabic Studies at the American University of Beirut. His research focuses on classical Arabic anthology literature, artistic prose, Sufism, and Maqamat. His publications include: Sufi Treatises of Abū ʿAbd al-Raḥmān al-Sulamī (al-Machreq, 2009); Sufi Inquiries and Interpretations by Abu ‘Abd al-Rahman al-Sulami (al-Machreq, 2010); Zad Safar al-Muluk of Abu Mansur al-Tha‘alibi (Klaus Schwarz Verlag, 2011), In the Shadow of Arabic (Brill, 2011), Sufism, Black and White (Brill, 2012), The Comfort of the Mystics (Brill, 2012) and several other articles and critical editions of Arabic texts.

    Top of Page


    Maurice Pomerantz is an Assistant Professor and Faculty Fellow at New York University. He has published numerous articles, book chapters and encyclopedia entries on both Classical Arabic literature and Islamic thought. His dissertation concerns the letters of the tenth century Iranian polymath and vizier, al-Sahib b. ‘Abbad. He has recently organised a conference on Courts and Performance in Middle East in Abu Dhabi with Evelyn Birge Vitz (NYU) and is engaged in preparing a critical edition of the Maqamat of al-Hamadhani with Bilal Orfali of the American University of Beirut. He is also working on a monograph on the subsequent history of the maqama genre.

    Top of Page


    Tahera Qutbuddin is Associate Professor of Arabic Literature at The University of Chicago. Her specialisation is in classical Arabic poetry and prose. Originally from Mumbai, India, she received her BA from Ain Shams University in Cairo, and her PhD from Harvard University. Her book titled Al-Mu’ayyad al-Shirazi and Fatimid Da‘wa Poetry: A Case of Commitment in Classical Arabic Literature was published by Brill in 2005. Her annotated edition and translation of A Treasury of Virtues: Sayings, Sermons, and Teachings of ‘Ali compiled by al-Qadi al-Quda’i, with the One Hundred Proverbs attributed to the compilation of al-Jahiz, will be published by New York University Press in the series Library of Arabic Literature in 2012. Professor Qutbuddin has also published several articles on Arabic literature, Fatimid-Isma‘ili-Tayyibi history and thought, and Arabic in India. Her current research project is titled Classical Arabic Oratory: The Rhetoric and Politics of Public Address in the Islamic World, for which she was selected as a Carnegie Scholar.

    Top of Page


    Andrew Rippin is Professor of Islamic History and former Dean (2000-2010) of the Faculty of Humanities at the University of Victoria, British Columbia, Canada. He is author of Muslims, their religious beliefs and practices, now in its fourth edition (London, 2011), and The Qur’an and its interpretative tradition (Aldershot, 2001). He is also the editor of several other books on the Qur’an and Islam, including The Blackwell Companion to the Qur’an (Oxford, 2005).

    Top of Page


    Nada Saab is Assistant Professor of Arabic studies in the Department of Humanities at the Lebanese American University in Beirut, and an affiliate of the Comparative Literature Program. Her research interests include medieval Sufi literature and theory, as well as the embodiments of this Sufi heritage in modern Arabic literature. Her critical editions of newly discovered medieval Sufi manuscripts and studies about their significance are forthcoming. She is also involved in the translation of modern Arabic dramatic works to English.

    Top of Page


    Jens Scheiner is Junior Professor and Leader of the Junior Research Group “Revelation, Reason, and Identity: Education in Early and Classical Islam (from the seventh to the thirteenth century CE)” at Georg-August-Universitāt Göttingen. In 2004 he gained his university degree (MA) from the University of Tübingen, Germany, where Jens Scheiner studied Islamic Studies, Social- and Economic History and Public Law. Jens Scheiner received his PhD from Radboud Universiteit Nijmegen, The Netherlands, writing a source-analytical study on the Islamic conquest of Damascus. Between 2008 and 2010 he was Lecturer and Research Assistant at the Institut for Islamic Studies at Freie Universität Berlin. Jens Scheinerʼs focus of research lies on the history and culture of the Middle East from Late Antiquity to classical Islamic times.

    Top of Page


    Steffen Stelzer is Professor of Philosophy and Chair of the Department of Philosophy at the American University in Cairo. He received his PhD from the Freie Universität Berlin, conducted research at the École Normal Supérieure in Paris and at Harvard, and taught at Johns Hopkins University. His areas of specialisation are: comparative analyses of Western and Islamic concepts of knowledge and of the transmission of knowledge, Rationality and Revelation and the ethical dimensions of Sufism.

    Top of Page


    Devin J. Stewart received a BA in Near Eastern Studies from Princeton University in 1984 and a PhD in Arabic and Islamic Studies from the University of Pennsylvania in 1991. He is Associate Professor of Arabic and Islamic Studies in the Department of Middle Eastern and South Asian Studies at Emory University. His writings focus on the Qur’an, Shiite Islam, biography and autobiography, medieval Arabic literature, and Arabic dialects. He is the author of ‘Saj‘ in the Qur’an: Prosody and Structure’. Journal of Arabic Literature 21 (1990), pp. 101-39 and ‘Poetic License in the Qur’an: Ibn al-Sa’igh al-Hanafi's Ihkam al-Ray fi Ahkam al-Ay.’ Journal of Qur’anic Studies 11.1 (2009), pp. 1-54.

    Top of Page


     
    Daryoush Mohammad Poor has recently submitted his PhD dissertation at the University of Westminster in Political and Social Studies. His thesis assesses how the tripartite ideal types of Max Weber can be used to describe and analyse issues relating to leadership and authority in the Muslim world, with particular attention to the Aga Khan Development Network. He is currently Editorial Researcher for the Encyclopaedia Islamica Project at The Institute of Ismaili Studies. He also teaches various courses on Shiʿi history, Fatimid intellectual history, and Shiʿi and Sufi texts.
