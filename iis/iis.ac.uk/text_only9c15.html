    Translating the Qur’an in an Age of Nationalism: Print Culture and Modern Islam in Turkey
    Mr M. Brett Wilson


    Oxford University Press (OUP) in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, November 2014.

    ISBN (Hardback): 978-0-19-871943-4
    • Synopsis
    • Contents
    • Index
    Synopsis

    Download Full Introduction

    Download Bibliography

    Translating the Qur’an in an Age of Nationalism: Print Culture and Modern Islam in Turkey addresses the question of how and why Muslims’ attitudes towards the printing and translation of the Qur’an shifted, during the period spanning the late Ottoman Empire and the early Turkish Republic, from one of rejection to one of acceptance.

    Since print media is crucial to the popularisation of Qur’an translations, the examination of print history and the attitudes towards print technology serves as the introductory focus for Translating the Qur’an in an Age of Nationalism. Chapters 1 and 2 thus present the history of the printing of the Qur’an in the late Ottoman Empire; both draw on materials from the Ottoman archives that provide an account of how the Ottomans came to print the Qur’an and how printed copies helped to bring about the broader shift towards Qur’anic accessibility.

    Following these prefatory remarks on printing, there is an overview, in Chapter 3, of the rise of Turkish as an Islamic language via the Turkish-language Qur’anic commentaries that became prevalent in the latter half of the nineteenth century. This chapter additionally probes the ethos of vernacular access – the notion that Muslims should have access to renderings in their own language – that crystallised at the turn of the century.

    The background for the two key themes of the book – the printing of the Qur’an and the translation of the Qur’an – having been provided, Chapter 4 embarks on a discussion of the translation debates from 1908 to 1918. These are revealed in this chapter through the varied perspectives of the prominent Egyptian writer Muhammad Rashid Rida (1865–1935), Christian missionaries and the Russian Tatar reformist Musa Carullah Bigiyev (1875–1949). The chapter, moreover, expands to consider the relationship between translation, narratives of progress and emerging discourses of Turkish nationalism.

    Next follows an exploration, in Chapter 5, of the controversial translations published during the early years of the Republic of Turkey. These translations dismayed the public, due to their uneven quality, and the resulting outcry instigated the state to sponsor an official translation project in response.

    However, before reflecting on the progress of the state-sponsored translation project, the book shifts its gaze in Chapter 6 to broader geographical horizons. This chapter looks at how Arab, South Asian and English Muslims wrestled with the question of translation in the period following the Turkish Qur’an controversy; it also examines the emergence of seminal English-language translations in the light of Turkish debates. On the heels of the developments in Turkey, Egyptian scholars reignited the debate about the necessity of translations to properly represent and spread Islam to the rest of the world.

    In Chapter 7, the focus returns to Turkey with the examination of that state’s sponsored translation project, its experiments with Turkish-language ritual and the difficulties that it encountered in attempting to create an official translation.

    Chapter 8 is largely a summational chapter which reviews the key points and discussions of the book. However, it also takes a moment to reflect on how the translation and publication of the Qur’an has come to be embraced today as means of addressing and influencing the widest number of Muslims possible (as reflected by the King Fahd Complex’s extensive publishing and translation activities), and as means of proselytisation (as reflected in the amplified missionary use of translations).
