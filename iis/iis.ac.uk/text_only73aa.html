    Modes


    Musician playing the Tar, Ink and Opaque watercolour, Iran, Qajar 20th century
    © Aga Khan Trust for Culture, Collection: Arts of the Book: Illustrated Texts, Miniatures
    The tradition of Persian music embodies twelve modal systems, known as dastgahs. Each dastgah represents a complex of skeletal melodic models on the basis of which a performer produces extemporised pieces. The twelve basic modes consist of seven primary modes (dastgah) and five secondary modes (avaz). 

    The seven main dastgahs, or ‘modes’ are: Shur, Mahur, Homayoun, Se-gah, Chahar-gah, Nava, and Rast Panj-gah.

    Mode
    ShurShur is, in some respects, considered by some musicians to be the most important of the dastgahs. Shur means ‘emotion’ and the study of radif traditionally begins with it (radifis a collection of old melodies that have been imparted from masters to pupils through generations).

    MahurMahur is another old mode. The radif of Mahur contains a great number of modulations that constitute digressions in Shur, Homayoun, and Bayat-i Isfahan, making it less homogeneous and a more varied mode compared to other modes. Mahur is a mode which is generally associated with happiness and is used in festivities and on joyful occasions. Yet, the modulations sometimes reach very passionate and plaintive stages that bring it closer to Rast Panj-gah with some gushas common with Mahur.

    HomayounHomayoun is one of the lengthiest dastgahsand is very popular in Persian music. It follows a scale that is very current in Eastern music. It is very poignant in character.

    Se-gahSe-gah is derived from a very old mode; its structure is very balanced because of its special scale. The similarities between the modes of Se-gah and Afshari are so striking that they are often considered by music experts as manifestations of a single modal structure.

    Chahar-gahChahar-gah is believed to be a mode with ancient origins. The radif of Chahar-gah probably inspired the composition of the Se-gah. Its manner of development and gushes are practically identical to those of Se-gah, but it follows a different scale. Chahrgah is epic in character and conveys a sentiment of glory.

    NavaThis is a very old mode, rarely used in popular or light music because it is considered part of classical music. It is the gentlest of all dastgahs.

    Rast-PanjgahThe word rast means ‘straight’ or ‘just’. In many Eastern traditions, this is deemed a fundamental and ‘perfect’ mode. There are virtually no rhythmic compositions in this mode. For this reason, all of the compositions that follow its scale have been attributed to Mahur.
