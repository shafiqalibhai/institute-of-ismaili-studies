IIS Scholar Presents Paper at Cambridge University
May 2011

Dr. Yahia Baiza of IIS’ Central Asian Studies Unit presented a paper entitled, Madrasa Education in Afghanistan: A Choice between Reform and Obliteration at a conference on Reform in Islamic Education at Cambridge University.
 

Dr. Baiza’s paper included an analysis of the rise and decline of Muslim scholarship and education, exploring the plight and reform of madrasa education in Afghanistan, and the fact that it is now faced with either reform or obliteration. Drawing on questions of why the madrasa system is unable to meet the educational, scientific and economic demands of the modern era in Afghanistan, he examined how this model of education, which in the early period of Islam represented the hallmark of Muslim education and civilizations, has become degraded to the extent that in the modern period it became a symbol of backwardness and that countries like Afghanistan, where the madrasa system has previously produced beacons of knowledge, has had to opt for a different educational model.

In his analysis, Dr. Baiza reviewed the background to the tradition of madrasa education in Afghanistan. In late 1870s, Amir Sher Ali founded a civil and a military school during the second term of his reign as Amir (r. 1868-1878) in Afghanistan. The foundation of the two schools marked a minor but symbolically significant rupture with traditional madrasa education. At this stage, it was not the actual curriculum, but the model and vision behind these schools that differentiated them from the madrasa system. The civil school was patterned on the British educational model in India, and the military school was even supervised by a British army general. Although these tentative changes in education were then destroyed during the Second Anglo-Afghan War (1878-1881), they left a legacy of a new form of education. Schools were restarted at the dawn of the next century and gradually sidelined madrasas from the mainstream formal education system. The schools were called makatib-e ‘asri (modern schools), and the newly founded form of education came to be known as talimat-e ‘asri (modern education).

Dr. Baiza argues that madrasa education can play a positive role in the present educational reconstruction and in helping us to understand social cohesion in Afghanistan. Debating the reform of madrasa education and implications for a new direction, Dr. Baiza’s paper illustrated how Afghanistan’s experience may help us to understand and explain the situation and contribute to the increasing demand for the reform of madrasa education. Dr. Baiza argues that Muslims’ scholarship and education is subject to continuous change and development. His paper recommends that as the madrasa system in Afghanistan has currently little option other than to embrace reform, the ground should be prepared for a critical and philosophical debate on the purpose and objective of knowledge and education.

The system can no longer afford to keep the doors of madrasas closed and reject critical, rational and philosophical reasoning and debate on religious sciences and meaningful participation of non-religious subjects in madrasa education. Dr. Baiza recommends that such debates have to focus, firstly, on what counts as knowledge and how it shall be defined in today’s context. Dr. Baiza acknowledges that it is a difficult, but inevitable task if madrasa education is to be transformed into a productive and well organized system, and, above all, to be saved from further degradation. His paper concluded with a set of recommendations for future research.

In the past, Dr Baiza has spoken at international conferences and seminars on educational research methodology, educational challenges and opportunities for Afghan refugees in Pakistan, challenges of educational reform in Afghanistan, rethinking madrasa education, and the emerging identity among the Afghan Ismaili community in Germany.
