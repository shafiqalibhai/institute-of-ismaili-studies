Contemplation and Action: the Spiritual Autobiography of a Muslim Scholar
Dr Sayyad Jalal Badakhchani


A New Edition and English translation of Sayr wa Suluk by Nasir al-Din Tusi, London: I.B. Tauris in assocation with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 1998, pp. xiii + 140.

ISBN (Hardback): 1 85043 908 7
ISBN (Paperback): 1 86064 523 2

  • Contents
  • Bibliography
Synopsis

Publication page on Google Books

Download PDF version of Introduction (195 KB)

This book is a translation of the autobiography of the celebrated Muslim scholar and theologian Nasir al-Din Tusi (d. 1274), one of the most important Islamic thinkers of the 13th century. Presented here is an English translation with a new, critically prepared edition of the Persian text of Tusi’s autobiography Sayr wa Suluk, accompanied by an Introduction by its editor/translator Dr. S. J. Badakhshani, along with the first translation of this text into English.

Badakhshani’s introduction is rich with copious detail on Tusi’s life, and strives to sketch an esoteric self-portrait of this philosopher whose exoteric theological thought and Peripatetic philosophical writings have all too often been the focus of scholarship. Previously veiled in robes of pious dissimulation, draped in the garb of taqiyya, the Ismaili spiritual orientation of Tusi is stressed here. The editor also provides a thorough discussion of Tusi’s other Ismaili works that he composed during his three-decade stay in the Nizari fortress communities in the Caspian region Tawalla wa tabarra, Matlub almu’minin, Rawda-yi taslim, as well as a number of his other minor Ismaili treatises. Attention should be drawn to his candid and well-researched discussion of Tusi’s celebrated collaboration with the Mongols on the one hand and his comparison of Tusi with Ghazali on the other.

The English translation of this difficult Persian text, full of recondite terminology and Ismaili arcana, is generally quite fluid and sometimes, even clearer than the original. This text runs 31 pages (pp. 23-54), divided into 54 paragraphs, corresponding to the same divisions found in the Persian text of the Sayr wa suluk.

The Persian text, filling 22 pages at the end of the book, is described in the “Note on the Text” (p. xii) as “an improved version of the text,” obtained by collating a previously published Persian edition of the text edited by the Iranian scholar Mudarris Radawi with various other works of Tusi wherein many of the same topics were discussed.

The book features a considerable technical apparatus, with some 65 “Notes to the Introduction” stretching 10 pages (55-65), and some 52 “Notes to the Translation” stretching six pages (66-71). Although the notes entirely support Tusi’s Ismaili affiliation and persuasion (cf. notes 1, 8, 99, pp. 66-67), taking for granted his support for the cause of the Nizari Ismaili Imams of Alamut, the translator’s erudition counterbalances any possibility of bias, so that at the very least, the Ismaili angle is argued with eloquence, scholarly insight and intelligence. There is also a substantial bibliography (72-77) and an index (pp. 78-86), featuring proper names, technical terms (both in Persian, Arabic and English) as well as many of the prominent place names found in the translation.

The idea of a constant, ever-living incarnation of knowledge in a living Imam, that hallmark of Ismaili epistemology, is a basic doctrine defended by Tusi all throughout this autobiography. Rejecting the idea and possibility that knowledge of God can be attained by purely rational and reflective means (“no rationalist can come to know God” §38), Tusi thinks that “Knowledge of God is [through] knowledge of the Imam” (§42; also §29). Tusi advocates complete and uncompromising submission to the Imam (§42), frowning on all forms of investigation of religion independent of the Imam (§29, 44, 46). If a man questions the Imam’s wisdom in any matter, this can only be due to “some imperfection in his essence, some illness in his soul, or some incompleteness in his beliefs,” (§48) and never because the Imam is subject to any ordinary human error. Salvation comes through faith alone.

For the history of Islamic theology, perhaps the most significant aspect of Contemplation and Action is that it finally and definitely establishes the pre-eminence of Ismaili spirituality in both Tusi’s thought and life.
