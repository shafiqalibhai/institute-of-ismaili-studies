Message to the participants

The purpose of The Summer Programme on Islam is to provide participants with a social, cultural and civilisational introduction to Islam as well as to situate the Ismaili tariqah within the larger Shi‘i and Sunni contexts of the intellectual and historical development of Islam.

One of the main objectives of the Summer Programme on Islam is to raise questions, stimulate reflection and encourage discussion on contemporary issues facing Muslim societies. In order to achieve this and for the Programme to be an enjoyable and stimulating experience both for participants and faculty members, it is extremely important that all participants are well-prepared for the sessions. This means that participants should have completed the readings set in advance of their arrival in Cambridge having read actively, and with a critical and engaged mind.

Participants should come to each session with questions, either about specific ideas in the readings or about the topic more generally. The faculty will expect them to have a good understanding of the content of the readings that have been set. The organisers are aware that entrants to the Programme are coming from diverse academic and professional backgrounds and therefore may be encountering much of this information for the first time. The important thing is that each participant gives themselves enough time to work through the readings at their own pace before arriving in Cambridge. When participants arrive, they should all feel reasonably comfortable with the material set in advance so that in the sessions all participants can truly engage with the perspectives and learning which the faculty have to offer.

To complement the academic sessions, participants can look forward to many other activities that will enable them to get to know their fellow participants and to explore the beautiful city of Cambridge. The Programme this year will be held in the beautiful and historic environment of Lucy Cavendish College, which is located in Cambridge.

If there are any queries, please do not hesitate to get in touch with us.

We wish you a safe journey and look forward to meeting you in the next few weeks time.

Morning

After the Prophet: Authority, Legitimacy and Institutionalisation I by Dr Nadia Eboo-Jamal and Dr Alnoor Dhanani

Objective(s):

  1. Survey the transition from Prophetic to post-Prophetic authority and the formation of institutions of authority in religious traditions in general and in the particular case of Islam
  2. Explore the institutions of leadership: caliphate; imamate; the emergence of the Shia understanding of post-Prophetic leadership and authority
  3. Explore the movement to preserve the memory of the Prophet through the formation of the institution of the sunna and how this crystallises into the institution of the ulama as custodians of Prophetic tradition

Afternoon

After the Prophet: Authority, Legitimacy and Institutionalisation II by Dr Alnoor Dhanani and Dr Nadia Eboo-Jamal

Objective(s):

  1. Explore the development and institutionalisation of fiqh/sharia in the Sunni and Shia contexts
  2. Examine the adaptation of fiqh/sharia to changing contexts
  3. Explore charismatic authority of Sufi pirs/shaykhs as a form of authority and the reasons for the development of the institution of Sufi tariqas

Morning

From the historical to the modern Dr Alnoor Dhanani

Objective(s):

  1. Survey the Muslim world from Napoleon’s invasion of Egypt to Ataturk’s embrace of Western secularism
  2. Explore Revival, Renewal and Reform movements from 18th until early 20th century
  3. Explore implications of European colonisation, anti-colonial nationalism, “modern” forms of national elites and the formation of new nation states
  4. Explore the shrinkage of classical fiqh/sharia to the domain of only “family law” and the adoption of Western legal codes

Contemporary Concerns: Islamism, Human Rights, Gender by Dr Nadia Eboo-Jamal

Objective(s):

  1. Situate the rise of Islamism (e.g. the Iranian Revoluton, jihad in Afghanistan, Talibans, Salafis, Wahhabis): the role of jihad, sharia and the Islamic State, women and non-Muslim minorities
  2. Explore the opposition to Islamist movements (such as spiritual brotherhoods, secularists)
  3. Explore the impact of globalisation

Biographies

Faculty

Dr Alnoor Dhanani

Dr Alnoor Dhanani specialises in the intellectual history of Islamic civilisation, in particular the interactions between science, theology, and philosophy in historical and contemporary contexts. He has taught courses related to the study of Islam at various institutions including Harvard, Tuftsand Brown University and at The Institute of Ismaili Studies in London, where he was the Head of the Department of Graduate Studies. He is currently a research fellow at the Department of Near Eastern Languages and Cultures of Harvard University and is working on the history of atomism after Avicenna in the Islamic milieu. His other research projects are in the area of historical and contemporary natural philosophy and cosmology including how Muslim atomists explained personal identity following resurrection and contemporary theological speculation on the Big Bang theory of the origin of the universe.

Dr Aziz Esmail

Dr Aziz Esmail is the former Dean of The Institute of Ismaili Studies (1988-98) and, since 1998, has served as one of its Governors. Originally trained in philosophy, literature and religion, Dr Esmail has held lecturing and research positions at various American universities as well as the University of Nairobi, Kenya. He has been associated with The Committee on Social Thought at the University of Chicago as well as Harvard University’s Center for the Study of World Religions and Graduate School of Education. Dr Esmail has been a consultant to, among other institutions, the Humanities division of the Rockefeller Foundation. In addition to articles in journals, his writings include The Poetics of Religious Experience (I. B. Tauris in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 1996) and A Scent of Sandalwood (I. B. Tauris in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 2002).


In addition to his responsibilities as a Governor, Dr Esmail contributes to the Institute in an executive capacity, chiefly in the formulation of the intellectual strategies which inform the work of its various departments.

Dr Nadia Eboo Jamal

Dr Nadia Eboo-Jamal received her Ph.D. in Near Eastern Languages and Literature from New York University in 1996 and is a specialist in Persian history and culture in the period of Mongol rule, with a particular emphasis on the Ismaili communities of the time.

Over the past fifteen years, Dr Eboo-Jamal has been actively involved in varying capacities with the Ismaili Tariqah and Religious Education Boards in the United Kingdom and the United States, providing her expertise and assistance to teaching activities, developing educational materials and human resources and contributing to the Ismaili community’s diverse religious education programmes. She is currently a Senior Lecturer in the Graduate Studies Department and a member of staff in the Curriculum Studies Department at the Institute of Ismaili Studies.

She is the author of Surviving the Mongols: Nizari Quhistani and the Continuity of Ismaili Tradition in Iran (London: I.B. Tauris in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 2002) and is currently engaged in several projects, including a translation and critical edition of Nizari Quhistani’s “Safarnama” and a critical edition and translation of a manuscript entitled, “Kitab Ta’wil al-Shari’a” a work on Fatimid Ismaili thought as presented by Imam al-Mui‘zz li-Din Allah and Qadi al-Nu‘man.

Mr Khalil Shariff

Mr Khalil Shariff joined Aga Khan Foundation Canada as Chief Executive Officer in August 2005. He was previously with the Toronto office of McKinsey & Company, an international management consultancy, where he advised governments, financial institutions, and health care providers on strategy, organisation, and operational improvement.

Khalil served on AKFC’s National Committee for five years, and has cultivated his interest in international development and conflict resolution issues through a variety of activities including as: Deputy Editor-in-Chief, Harvard Negotiation Law Review; Policy Co-ordinator and Research Associate, Harvard Program on Humanitarian Policy and Conflict Research; Legal Intern, Chambers of the Vice-President, International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda in Arusha, Tanzania; Intern, Office of Under-Secretary-General, UN Department of Peacekeeping Operations. He was the youngest member ever elected as a School Trustee in 1993 for the Board of School Trustees in Richmond, B.C.

Khalil holds a B.A. in International Relations and Economics from the University of British Columbia and a J.D. magna cum laude from the Harvard Law School.

‘Anytime Day Single’: can be used any time of day. Anytime fares are fully flexible walk-up tickets with no restrictions on when you can travel. ‘Anytime Day Single’ tickets are valid for travel on any train only on the date shown on the ticket.

Getting to London King’s Cross St Pancras Station:

At Heathrow Airport, follow signs for the Underground. Take the Eastbound Piccadilly Line towards ‘Arnos Grove’ or ‘Cockfosters’ to King’s Cross St. Pancras underground station. Please alight from the underground train at King’s Cross St. Pancras underground station. The journey time from Heathrow Airport to King’s Cross St. Pancras underground station will take up to approximately one hour and would cost approximately £5. You could also take Heathrow Express to London Paddington – which will cost you around £20 – getting you to Paddington in 15 minutes. From Paddington, you could take Bakerloo line towards Elephants and Castle and alight at Baker Street. From Baker Street, you will need to take Metropolitan, Hammersmith or Circle line towards King’s Cross, alighting at King’s Cross station. This is a 15 minutes journey and will cost you around £4.

At Gatwick Airport, take the First Capital Connect Bedford train, which runs directly from Gatwick Airport to London St. Pancras. They run every 15 minutes with an approximate 48 minutes journey time. A single journey ticket is around £10. From London St. Pancras station please walk across to London King’s Cross station (this takes approximately five minutes).

Alternatively, from Gatwick Airport, participants may wish to take the Gatwick Express train to Victoria station – which will cost you around £15. From Victoria, take the Northbound Victoria Line Underground to King’s Cross St. Pancras underground station. The final destination on the underground train should read ‘Walthamstow Central’ or ‘Seven Sisters’. Please alight from the underground train at King’s Cross St. Pancras. The journey time from Victoria underground station to King’s Cross St. Pancras is approximately 10 minutes and will cost you around £4.
