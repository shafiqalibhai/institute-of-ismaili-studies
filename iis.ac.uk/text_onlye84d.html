    Fragmentation and Compilation: The Making of Religious Texts in Islam
    A Comparison with Ancient Mesopotamia, Judaism and Christianity
    29-30 May 2012
    The Institute of Ismaili Studies, London
    2nd Floor, Room 2.1
    Convenor: Asma Hilali

    Workshop description
    Workshop programme

    Abstracts


    Fragmentation and Compilation: The Human Free Will as an Example in Hadith Literature
    Viviane Comerro-de Prémare, Institut National de Langues et Civilisations Orientales, Paris

    What can be said about the existence of evil in the world? And about justice of an almighty God? These questions arose among Muslim traditionists, who answered by taking a stand in the debate of the time about divine predestination and man’s free will. Some of them used biblical figures and in particular that of Ezra, known as ‘Uzayr in the Islamic tradition, to make him the first prophet to question God about predestination. This paper seeks to go beyond the anecdotal setting of the stories to reveal the implicit underlying theology, bearing in mind that this theology does not express itself in a speculative mode but in a narrative one, and that the answer is not always the same.

    These stories are presented in the form of a literary compilation of hadiths. One might consider that a hadith is an autonomous and meaningful textual unit and, as such, it should be transmitted more or less literally as part of an inherited tradition. Yet, this autonomous textual unit can take a different meaning when regarded as a part of a compilation. To illustrate this point, the paper analyses an example taken from one of the oldest collections of prophetic stories, the one by Wathima al-Farisi (d. 851 CE), in the section dedicated to the Prophet ‘Uzayr.

    Practice – Teaching – Tradition: Constructing the Authority of Sunni Islamic Religious Texts
    Stefan Leder, Orient Institut Beirut

    The transmission of utterances is a main instrument for constructing authority in Sunni Islamic religious texts. This paper discusses some implications of this conceptual framework with respect to notions of knowledge and authority. Looking into the process of tradition-building as it occurs in a side branch of Sunni teaching, we detect what seems to be at first glance a sequence of layers of redaction: a certain fluidity of textual appearance at an early stage, and a gradual fixation of the text in the course of more systematic scholarly transmission subsequently. However, this is not a general evolutionary development; fluidity of the authoritative texts used in quotation does not disappear with time, and both ‘layers’ may coexist as they are related to different scholarly milieus. This paper observes that close-to-oral transmission occurs in connection with the formation of teachings still struggling for the recognition of certain practices. In contrast, canonisation tends to isolate the text (utterance) from its contested context and to turn it into a freely utilisable component of compilations.

    On the Consumption of Law in Modern Central Asia: Who Cares for Doctrinal Citations in the Fatwas?
    Paolo Sartori, Austrian Academy of Sciences, Vienna

    Responsa literature (fatawa) is commonly treated as a body of scholarship which reflects the hermeneutic task of the mufti alone. This approach to the study of fatawa depends, at least partly, on the influence that adab al-mufti literature had on the studies of Islamic law. Indeed Islamic legal theory distinguishes responsa from judgments (ahkam) since the former are mainly informative and communicative in nature (akhbari). On this ground, muftis have been equated with transmitters of hadiths or translators. Prior to the Soviet period, fatawa were delivered in Central Asia (taken here as a case study) simply by answering ‘yes’ or ‘no’ to a question (istifta’) written in the vernacular language; the latter took the shape of a text (matn) in Persian or Chaghatay Turki, which was complemented by fragmented citations (riwaya) of doctrinal authorities in Arabic. The question was formulated in such a way as to lead the mufti to issue the answer desired by its applicant. In other words, the legal opinion, which the mufti could either accept or reject, was already embedded in the text of the question. The citations of doctrinal authorities served as evidence in support of the view expressed in the question. That is, the vernacular text should show conformity to the citations in Arabic.

    This paper applies an approach to the study of fatawa which treats the latter as legal speech acts. This entails close investigation of the authorial intentions behind the production of the texts. In the case of fatawa, this approach calls for recovering the agency of the applicants of the legal opinions (mustafti). The paper argues that, in modern Central Asia, legal questions and the opinions embedded therein were not always formulated in keeping with doctrinal references. By contrast, it appears that the demands of the mustafti could substantially affect the hermeneutic task of the mufti.

    Citation, Appropriation and Re-Shaping in the Reception History of the Qur’an. The Epistles of the Pure Brethren (Rasa’il Ikhwan al-Safa’) as a Case in Point
    Omar Alí-de-Unzaga, The Institute of Ismaili Studies, London

    Citations from the Qur’an are one of the most distinctive characteristics of numerous genres in Islamic intellectual history. This paper explores the mechanisms that are in place when quotations from a Scripture are used in a text. In particular it focuses on the quotations of the Qur’an in the Epistles of the Pure Brethren (Rasa’il Ikhwan al-Safa’), as a representative of the intellectual production of what can be termed ‘humanistic’ literature in the tenth century CE. However, while it is relatively simple to find how a Qur’an commentator (mufassir) interpreted a specific verse, it is much more complex and difficult to see the interpretive process that takes place through the phenomenon of citation. Citations are insertions of textual units originating in one text into a larger text, which serves as its new ‘home’.

    As such, the textual unit takes on a new life, and, in the new co-text, a new meaning. By their very nature, citations are fragmentary; this paper further explores whether the Qur’an is fragmentary or whether it has been made to appear as fragmentary by the very phenomenon of citations (and exegesis in general). The main point of the paper is to explore how citations of religious texts contribute to the re-shaping of their reception – once readers see the quotation of a Qur’anic verse as inserted into a work and incorporated into the argument, their reading of that verse in the ‘home’ text (i.e. the Qur’an) will be coloured by the interpretation underlying and surrounding the quotation. In the case of the Epistles, this paper analyses certain examples of citation as appropriation.

    Athens, Jerusalem, Mecca: The Patristic Crucible of the Abrahamic Religions
    Guy Stroumsa, University of Oxford

    From Justin Martyr in the second century CE to John of Damascus in the eighth, six centuries of early Christian theological literature offer one of the most impressive literary monuments of late antiquity. From its first steps as a Jewish heresy and a persecuted religio illicita, through Constantinian triumphalism to its becoming, in the Near East, a religion of tolerated dhimmis in the Islamic realm, early Christianity also offers the key to the crystallisation of what would become, with the emergence of Islam, ‘the Abrahamic religions.’ This paper argues that it is with early Christianity that the main elements of the conflict regarding the inheritance of Abraham and his ‘true’ religion were formed. In that sense, early Muslim thinkers applied to Christians the ways the Christians, who called themselves verus Israel (true Israel), had themselves employed towards the Jews. It is thanks to the Abrahamic moment of late antiquity that the cultural and religious ecosystem, which would extend in the Middle Ages from Baghdad to Cordoba, and beyond, was formed.

    Conceptualising Books in Mesopotamia: The Composition of Extispicy Treatises in the Old-Babylonian Period
    Jean-Jacques Glassner, Centre Nationale de la Recherche Scientifique, Paris

    From the eighteenth century BCE on, Mesopotamian diviners began to compose treatises in the field of extispicy (divination through the inspection of animal entrails). This scientific project, which seems to have begun during or around the reign of Hammurabi of Babylon (1792-1750 BCE), was not a short-lived movement. On the contrary, it was a long-lasting work which was carried out for several centuries and conducted with rigorous scientific methodology. Copying was not the sole task of the scribes; some of them composed books. This raises the question of how Mesopotamian scribes wrote books, and how they went from the act of copying to that of composing; from reproduction to creation. To try to answer this question, documents must be identified in which one can locate a project of creation. Such manuscripts, one expects, are constituted with files and more or less long excerpts or compilations. Many of these sources have disappeared and a systematic study of this preliminary work is therefore impossible.

    The process of using excerpts is an exact continuation of scholarly activity. Scribes might sometimes include collections of unconnected passages, such as some proverbs on tablets belonging to the first level of education or some excerpts of literary texts, each selected passage being separated from the others by a single or a double rule. The same method was in use in extispicy, which was a subject definitively not taught at school during the first level cycle.

    Modelling the Paleo-Muslim Qur'an
    Aziz al-Azmeh, Central European University, Budapest

    The paleo-Muslim Qur’an, as composed, recomposed, canonised and received in the last two decades of Prophet Muhammad’s lifetime and in the decades immediately following, presents an especially propitious instance that might be considered in the discussion of this workshop’s overarching theme, that of fragmentation and compilation. Fragmentary textual elements that eventually constituted the canonical ‘Uthmanic redaction, and other, ‘apocryphal’ redactions as well, resulted from declamation, cantillation, repetition and reiteration, self-reflexive semantic and commentarial expansion, interpolation, occasional adaptation and reformulation, narrative and allusive reference to contemporary and non-contemporary events, and arguably intertextual references to both Qur’anic and extra-Qur’anic texts, in both written and the spoken media. The consideration of the physical state of the Qur’anic texts, both codicological and morphological, and of Qur’anic variants, allows for modelling these feedback processes of canonising composition and eventual redaction.

    Typology of Variants in the San‘a Palimpsest (Manuscript 27.1 Dar al-Mahtutat)
    Asma Hilali, The Institute of Ismaili Studies, London

    One important issue which arises out of examining the oldest manuscript of the Qur’an (the San‘a palimpsest) is that of Qur’anic variants. The variants can be discerned in fragments of the Qur’anic text, and sometimes have an impact on determining the meaning of the verses in question. This paper analyses the phenomena pertaining to these variants in the context of the history of transmission, and raises the following questions: Do the variants correspond to, or arise out of, a definitive system of composition? Is there a discernible relationship between the fragmented aspect of the manuscript and the system of variants? What does the system of variants tell us about the use of the manuscript? Can we consider the variants as constituting a specific and definable dimension of religious textual composition? My approach aims to go beyond the framework of the issue of the ‘Uthmanic or non-‘Uthmanic Qur’an codex, by investigating the ways in which the processes of compilation and fragmentation might serve as keys for a better understanding of the different levels of meaning within religious texts; and by trying to ascertain the degree to which these levels of meaning might be related to the historical modalities of textual construction and re-construction.
