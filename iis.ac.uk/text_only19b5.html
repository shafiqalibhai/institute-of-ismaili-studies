Introductory Remarks
Dr Amyn B Sajoo

 April 2009

Welcome to this second lecture in our series ‘Talking Ethics’ – and the first public lecture at the Institute’s new home on Euston Road.

We started off this series with a colourful historical account by Armando Salvatore of how the emerging public sphere in Muslim settings was shaped by competing understandings of the ‘common good’ as a driving force. The ethics of the common good have found particular expression in the idea of maslaha – which has given scope to Muslim jurists, philosophers and political leaders to negotiate tradition and modernity, secular and sacred, within the framework of a faith tradition instead of having to step outside it.

And this speaks to a dynamic that cuts across societies and civilisations: the nexus between law and ethics. It is a relationship that can swing from complementarity to tension and polarity. Without an ethical critique of public law and politics, which is what maslaha at its best provides, governance is at the mercy of dangerous choices. They can be driven by strictly utilitarian and material concerns, or at the opposite end, by purely ideological agendas.

This is no less true in international relations as it is in national politics; nor is this new, of course. For Muslims, the very beginning of empire after the passing of the Prophet brought home a consciousness about the need to subject political and military power to an ethical framework. Here is how the first Caliph, Abu Bakr, articulated the admonitions of the Qur’an and the Prophet about behaviour in conflict:

Do not act treacherously … Do not mutilate; do not kill children or old men, or women; do not cut off the heads of the palm-trees or burn them; do not cut down the fruit trees; do not slaughter a sheep or a cow or a camel, except for food. You will pass by people who devote their lives in cloisters; leave them and their devotions alone. You will come upon people who bring you platters in which are various sorts of food; if you eat any of it, mention the name of God over it.[i]

There is, of course, a constant gap between such ethical ideals and actual practice. The patron of this Institute, His Highness the Aga Khan, warned of this peril and suggested the path ahead, in his remarks at the Evora University Symposium in Portugal in 2006:
 

The resurgence of religious feeling – which should be such a positive force – can become a negative influence when it turns into self-righteousness. All of the world’s great religions warn against this excess – yet in the name of those same religions too many are tempted to play God themselves – rather than recognising their humility before the Divine … What I am calling for is an ethical sensibility which can be shared across denominational lines and which can foster a universal moral outlook.[ii]

In other words, walking away from religious traditions is hardly the answer. The challenge is to mobilise shared humanistic traditions without losing sight of the spiritual commitments that bind us all.

There are those who insist that religion only gets in the way of a ‘rational’ framework of public ethics, whether in civil society or in uncivil conflict. Yet it is worth recalling such key figures as from Desmond Tutu and the Dalai Lama to Shirin Ebadi, Martin Luther King and Gandhi, who have stood for a faith-based outlook that is universalist, not exclusive.

Indeed, one of our leading ethical thinkers, Alasdair MacIntyre, warns that an exclusively ‘secular’ rationality ignores the simple truth that all rationalities are ultimately lodged within traditions.[iii] It is traditions, religious and secular, that must find ways to dialogue; it is no use looking for some imagined abstraction that claims to stand outside of them.

To shed light on pressing, pluralist quest, I am privileged to welcome Professor Mervyn Frost of King’s College London.

Professor Frost was a Rhodes Scholar at Oxford, and held several lectureships in South Africa, where he also received his early education. In 1996 he was back in the UK as Professor of International Relations at the University of Kent at Canterbury. He now heads the Department of War Studies at King’s College London.

Professor Frost is the author of numerous books, including Ethics in International Relations (Cambridge, 1996), Constituting Human Rights: Global Civil Society and the Society of Democratic States (Routledge, 2002), and most recently, Global Ethics: Anarchy, Freedom and International Relations (Routledge, 2009).

References


[i] Quoted in John Alden Williams, ed. Themes of Islamic Civilization, University of California Press, 1972, p. 262.

[ii] Remarks at Evora University Symposium, ‘Cosmopolitan Society, Human Safety and Rights in Plural and Peaceful Societies’, Portugal, 12 February 2006: http://www.akdn.org/speech/228/Evora-University-Symposium
 
[iii] Whose Justice? Which Rationality? University of Notre Dame Press, 1988.
