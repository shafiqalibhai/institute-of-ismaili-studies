    IIS Alumnus Presents Paper at Chester University
    June 2009

    Yahia Baiza (Class of 2002) presented a paper in a conference on ‘Muslim Youth: Challenges, Opportunities and Expectations’, which was held from 20-22 March 2009 and was organized jointly by the Association of Muslim Social Scientists (AMSS UK), based in London, and the Department of Theology and Religious Studies of the University of Chester.

    The aim of the conference was to bring Muslim social scientists and those interested in the study of Islam and Muslim youth together in order to discuss current issues related to the life of Muslim youth, to encourage a better understanding of the Muslim youths’ circumstances, challenges and opportunities. In the second panel of the first day of the conference, ‘Muslim Youth: Identities’, Yahia Baiza presented the findings of his recent research on the issue of hybrid identity among the Afghan Ismaili youth in Germany.

    His study explores the role of religion, language and ethnicity in the formation of identity among the Afghan Ismaili community in the city of Essen in Germany. The Afghan Ismaili community in Germany are primarily concentrated in three major areas, which are defined according to the location of their religio-cultural centres called Jama‘atkhana (literally, house of gathering). These three areas include Essen, Frankfurt and Munich. The Afghan Ismaili community in Essen include those families that live in the city of Essen and in the neighbouring cities, from where they visit the Essen Jama‘atkhana for prayer, attending social events and gathering, religious education classes called Bait al-‘Ilm (literally, the House of Knowledge), and other religious and cultural festivals. Population-wise, the Essen area marks the largest concentration of Afghan Ismaili community in Germany.

    In his study, Yahia examined the role and influence of religion, language and ethnicity on the formation of identity among the Afghan Ismaili youth. The findings demonstrated that the Afghan Ismaili youth has developed a hybridised identity by mixing and matching parentally and community given identity with the cultural values of the German society. Although the majority of the Afghan Ismaili youth who participated in this study have never been to Afghanistan, they have learnt about the country of their origin, living condition, family and ethnic status, reasons for their migration, political situation, etc., through their parents, community members, other Afghan fellows, and the public media.

    The Afghan Ismaili community in Germany is a young community in diaspora. The community members hold a strong interest in keeping their cultural and traditional values and identity alive. The findings of Yahia’s study demonstrated that the Afghan Ismaili youth are conscious of their ethnic background (Hazara), ethnic language (Hazaragi) and country of origin (Afghanistan) and the Farsi language, even though they have never been to their homeland. The findings have shown that the Afghan Ismaili youth appreciate their hostland’s cultural values but, at the same time, they have a commitment to maintain their parentally given identity. For example, they view the freedom of expression, liberty of thought, and educational opportunities as positive values from the German society. They have also been appreciative of the sense of collectiveness, hospitality, respect for elders, parents and the sense of sharing and caring as positive aspects of their homeland identity. The findings also demonstrated that when different fields, such as religion, language, ethnicity, and hostland’s values compete for dominance in determining the multiple identities, the research participants have resolved the competing fields by characterising themselves as an Ismaili Muslim, as their personal and communal identity. The findings showed that the Afghan Ismaili youth felt that they can always firmly hold to their religion, religious values and principles at any time, and that their religion is always there for them, while the same sense of trust cannot be given to them by their ethnic, linguistic and hostland’s values and identities.
