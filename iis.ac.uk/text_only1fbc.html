    Workshop: Cultural Responses to the Qur’an
    18 November 2011
    American Academy of Religion Annual Meeting (AAR), USA

    Workshop description
    Workshop programme
    Biographies
    Paper abstracts in order of presentation

     

    Nuha al-Sha‘ar is a Research Associate in the Qur’anic Studies Unit, Department of Research and Publications at The Institute of Ismaili Studies. She completed her PhD on Islamic ethics and political thought at the University of Cambridge. Her thesis title is “Abu Hayyan al-Tawhidi and Ethical Thought in the Fourth Century of Islam,” which she is currently revising for publication. With Verena Klemm, she co-edited “Sources and Approaches Across Disciplines in Near Eastern Studies: Proceedings of the 24th Congress of L'Union Européenne des Arabisants et Islamisants” (Peeters, Leuven, 2012).

    Carol Bier is visiting scholar at the Graduate Theological Union in Berkeley, CA (2010-12) and Research Associate at The Textile Museum in Washington, DC (2001-12), where she has served as Curator for Eastern Hemisphere Collections (1984-2001). Her research focuses on the historical development of Islamic patterns as intersections of geometry and art at a significant moment in the history of mathematics in the 11th century CE. Her publications include Science, Crafts, and the Production of Knowledge in Iran and Eastern Islamic Lands (a special issue of Iranian Studies, 41/4 [2008], co-edited with Elaheh Kheirandish and Najma al-Din Yousefi), The Persian Velvets at Rosenborg (Copenhagen, 1995), and Woven from the Soul, Spun from the Heart: Textile Arts of Safavid and Qajar Iran (Washington, 1987). She serves on the editorial board of the Journal of Mathematics and the Arts (London), and edited The Textile Museum Journal (1992-2002).

    Whitney S. Bodman is an Associate Professor of Comparative Religion at Austin Presbyterian Theological Seminary, as well as being an ordained minister. He completed his Th.D. in Comparative Religion and Islamic Studies at Harvard Divinity School and is the author of The Poetics of Iblis: Qur’anic Narrative as Theology (Cambridge, MA, 2007). He has been involved in interfaith organisations, including Ecumenical Clergy for the Middle East Crisis and the Massachusetts Conference of Churches Jewish-Christian and Muslim-Christian Dialogues. His current research focuses on the Qur’an, modern Islam in the Middle East, and rituals of sacred space and pilgrimage.

    Hasan M. El-Shamy is a Professor of Folklore (Folkloristics) in the Department of Folklore and Ethnomusicology, the Department of Near Eastern Languages and Cultures, and the African Studies Program at Indiana University. He received a B.A. with Honours in Arabic and Islamic Studies from Ain-Shams University in Cairo, Egypt. Later he received an M.A. in Folklore from Indiana University, as well as a Ph.D. in Folklore with Interdisciplinary training in Folklore, Psychology, and Anthropology from Indiana University. He is the author of Religion Among the Folk in Egypt (London, 2008); A Motif Index of The Thousand and One Nights (Bloomington, 2006); and Types of the Folktale in the Arab World: A Demographically Oriented Tale-Type Index (Bloomington, 2004). He co-edited with Jane Garry, Archetypes and Motifs in Folklore and Literature: A Handbook (Armonk, N. Y., 2005). El-Shamy has been awarded a number of honors for his contribution to the fields of Folklore and Middle Eastern studies, including the Great China Lecture Award from East China Normal University (2011) and the Senior Scholar Award from Institute of Advanced Studies in Anthropology, National Museum of Ethnology, Osaka, Japan (2002). He also has received the prestigious Chicago Folklore Prize (1980), has been a Fulbright Fellow (1960–1961, 1987–1988), and has been elected as a Fellow of the American Folklore Society (2006).

    Özgen Felek is a Mellon Postdoctoral Fellow in Religious Studies at Stanford University. She received her first Ph.D. from Firat University in Turkey, in classical Ottoman poetry with a focus on the Sebk-i Hindi (Indian Style) poetical movement (2007), and her second Ph.D. from the Near Eastern Studies Department at the University of Michigan with emphasis on Ottoman dream culture and Sufism (2010). With Walter G. Andrews, she is the co-editor of Victoria R. Holbrook’a armağan (Istanbul, 2006), a collection of essays in honor of Victoria Rowe Holbrook. With Alexander Knysh, she is also the co-editor of forthcoming Dreams and Visions in Islamic Societies (2012).

    Zareena Grewal is an Assistant Professor in the departments of American Studies and Religious Studies at Yale University. She is also the Director of the Center for the Study of American Muslims at the Institute for Social Policy and Understanding. Her book, Destination Tradition: American Islam and the Crisis of Authority (New York, forthcoming), explores debates about religious authority in US mosques.

    Andrew Rippin is Professor of Islamic History and former Dean (2000-2010) of the Faculty of Humanities at the University of Victoria, British Columbia, Canada. He is author of Muslims, their religious beliefs and practices, now in its fourth edition (London, 2011), and The Qur’an and its interpretative tradition (Aldershot 2001). He is also the editor of several other books on the Qur’an and Islam, including The Blackwell Companion to the Qur’an (Oxford, 2005).

    Nargis Virani is Assistant Professor of Arabic and Islamic Studies at The New School in New York. She received her MA and PhD from Harvard University. She has studied the Qur’an with the Shaykh of al-Azhar Mosque in Cairo and holds a shahada (certificate) and an ijaza (permission to teach the Qur’an). Her first book project entitled ‘I am the Nightingale of the Merciful: The Macaronic Rumi’, will analyse the mixed-language poems in Rumi’s Diwan. It will also include a translation into English of all of Rumi’s multilingual verses in Arabic, Persian, Turkish, Greek, and Armenian. Virani’s second book project is tentatively entitled ‘The Qur’an in Muslim Literary Memory’. She hopes to analyse the use of the Qur’an by a variety of ‘litterateurs’ from secular, religious, and mystical backgrounds.

    Clare Wild received an A.B. from Princeton University, a Licentiate in Arabic and Islamic Studies at the Pontifical Institute for the Study of Arabic and Islam (PISAI), and her Ph.D. from The Catholic University of America. After 10 years at Georgetown as both a research assistant and professorial lecturer, she is currently lecturing at the University of Auckland, New Zealand. She is also, with Kirill Dmitriev, co-editing Brill’s Handbook of Christian-Muslim Studies (forthcoming, 2013). The Institute of Ismaili Studies - Biographies
