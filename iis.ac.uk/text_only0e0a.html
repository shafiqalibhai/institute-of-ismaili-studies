    Music & Melodies of the Persian Ismaili Qasideh


    Introduction | Modes | Melodies | Musical Instruments | Qasideh Tradition | Qasideh Collection


    Qasideh Collection


    Ink, Opaque watercolour & gold on paper, Iran & India, Safavid and Mughal, 16th-17th century CE. © Aga Khan Trust for Culture, Collection: Arts of the Book: Illustrated Texts, Miniatures.
    This page contains a collection of Persian Qasidehs which can be accessed through the links provided in the list below. The Ismaili community has had a long history in Persia. Since the establishment of the Nizari state (483 AH / 1090 CE) in Persia till the migration of Imam Hasan Ali Shah, Aga Khan I, in 1257 AH / 1841 CE, Ismaili Imams lived in Persia for almost eight centuries. Inevitably, there has been a significant mutual influence between Ismaili literature and the wider Persian literary traditions. Persian Ismailis have used a wide range of Persian literature for their Qasideh tradition, as they share many of the common themes and ideas embedded in Persian literature and poetry in the wider society. 
    This collection of Qasidehs represents only a very small selection of Qasidehs which have been used and survived amongst the Ismailis of Persia.Because of the strong presence of the mystical tradition in Persia for centuries, it has had a significant impact on Persian literature and poetry. Furthermore, the Ismailis of Persia mainly lived under the guise of Sufi tariqas for a long time to avoid persecution after the sacking of Alamut by the Mongol hordes. Therefore, there has been a mutual interchange of ideas and terminologies between the Sufis and the Ismailis, resulting in many similarities between their poetry and literature. 
    Poetry, by its very nature, lends itself to different and pluralistic interpretations. However, when poetry is translated into another language, the translator inevitably has to interpret the intent of the poet. Hence, translation reflects one particular interpretation of the poem, to the exclusion of other equally valid interpretations. In the process of translating this collection of Persian Qasidehs, every effort has been made to translate the poems as accurately as possible. However, this translation should not be considered as the only possible meaning of the original poems in Persian.
