    Bab

    Farhad Daftary

    Tr. Rahim Gholami

    This is an edited version of an article that was originally published in Daʼirat al-maʻarif-i buzurg-i Islami, The Encyclopaedia Islamica, Vol 3, p.1000-1002, ed. Wilferd Madelung & Farhad Daftary, Brill (London, 2011)

    Bab was a high rank in the da‘wa organisation and religious hierarchy (hudud al-din) of the Ismailis. The hierarchical organisation of the Ismaili da‘wa and the functions of its different ranks were important but secret and obscure aspects of the Ismaili movement. Ismaili works generally maintain silence on the subject because of the esoteric and often secretive nature of the Ismaili da‘wa. The enmity of many Sunni amirs, rulers and religious scholars generally obliged the Ismailis to conduct their da‘wa activities in utmost secrecy (except within the dominions of the Fatimid state). Modern scholarship based on the recovery of numerous Ismaili texts has now shed much light on the organisation of the Ismaili da‘wa and its functioning, especially during the Fatimid period (see Daftary, The Isma‘ilis, 213–220; Halm, The Fatimids, 56 ff.).

    The title of Bab before and during the Fatimid period

    Before the Fatimid period the term bab (also known as bab al-abwab) was already used by the early Ismailis who then lived in complete secrecy; however, we do not know the exact meaning and application of the term during that early phase (see Jaʿfar b. Mansur al-Yaman, Kitab al-kashf, 24; idem, Kitab al-‘alim, text, 17, 24, 30, 32, 45). In a few Ismaili sources of the Fatimid period, where the hierarchy of the da‘wa is discussed, the term bab or bab al-abwab is usually used in reference to the executive head of the da‘wa, a position which came immediately under the imam. In Ismaili doctrine, the rank of bab is identical with the official title of da‘i al-du‘at (al-Qadi al-Nu‘man, 1/238, 250). The title of da‘i al-du‘at is frequently used in non-Ismaili sources, while it is rarely mentioned in the Ismaili texts. As an example, many Ismaili authors refer to al-Mu’ayyad fi al-Din al-Shirazi (d. 470 H/1078 CE) as the bab of the Fatimid caliph-imam al-Mustansir, whereas the non-Ismaili sources refer to him as the da‘i al-du‘at (Ibn Muyassar, 18; al-Maqrizi, Itti‘azz, 2/251).

    Significance of Bab al-abwab in Isamaili Da‘wa Organisation

    In some official correspondence (sijillat) of the Fatimid caliph-imam al-Mustansir with the Sulayhid Ismaili rulers in Yemen, the term da‘i al-du‘at was also used in reference to al-Mu’ayyad fi al-Din al-Shirazi as his official title (see al-Majdu‘, 40). Under the Fatimids, especially until caliph-imam al-Hakim’s reign (386–411 AH / 996–1021 CE), the position of the bab or da‘i al-du‘at and the post of the qadi al-qudat or chief qadi were held by the same person whose responsibility was to propagate Ismaili teachings as well as act as the chief judge of the Fatimid state (see Halm, The Fatimids, 26; al-Maqrizi, al-Khitat, 1/403).

    In other words, responsibility of enforcing and explaining the zahir, or the letter of the law, and interpreting its batin or inner meaning, were united in one and the same person under the overall guidance of the Ismaili imam of the time. In those official Fatimid ceremonials wherein the positions of the d‘ai al-du‘at and the qadi al-qudat were not invested in the same person the qadi al-qudat would take precedence over the da‘i al-du‘at or bab al-abwab (Ibn al-Tuwayr, 110–112; al-Qalqashandi, 3/483; al-Maqrizi, al-Khitat, 1/391). In many of his works the da‘i Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani (d. ca. 411 /1020) refers to the high status of the bab and his close relationship to the imam (pp. 135–136, 138, 205–208; see also al-Mu’ayyad fi al-Din, ed. Mustafa Ghalib, 3/202, 256–257; Nasir-i Khusraw, 35; Ivanow, 19–23).

    All Ismaili texts unanimously agree that the bab was one of the most devout and learned members of al-da‘wa al-hadiya (the rightly-guiding mission). This person was responsible for the execution of the overall policies of the da‘wa as specified by the Ismaili imam. During the Fatimid period in Ismaili history, the bab served at the centre of the da‘wa organisation which was located at the Fatimid capital, acting under the direct supervision of the imam.

    The Ismaili da‘wa in Regions during the Fatmid period

    The bab was aided by a number of subordinate da‘is at the da‘wa headquarters. He was responsible for conveying the teachings and instructions of the imam to the twelve hujjas who occupied a rank immediately below the bab in the hierarchy of the da‘wa. Each hujja was in charge of a major da‘wa region or jazira; and the world (presumably the parts falling outside the Fatimid state) was divided into twelve such regions on the basis of a variety of geographical and ethnic criteria. The bab played a major role in selecting the hujjas and da‘is of the regions (jaza’ir) beyond the Fatimid borders (see al-Qalqashandi, 8/239–241). Furthermore, the bab was also responsible for appointing the da‘is of different towns and regions within the Fatimid territories such as Sur, ‘Akka, Ramla and ‘Asqalan.

    As the executive head of the da‘wa, the bab was also responsible for compiling and delivering the weekly lectures known as the majalis al-hikma. These ‘sessions of wisdom’, which were held at a special chamber (also called majalis) in the Fatimid palace, were part of the intellectual traditions unique to the Ismailis and were specially designed for the benefit of those who had already been initiated into the da‘wa and had taken their oath of allegiance (‘ahd) to the Ismaili imam. These lectures essentially revolved around esoteric Ismaili doctrine known as hikma or wisdom. Therefore, the bab had to obtain the endorsement of the Ismaili imam for the contents of these lectures as the imam was the only source of this wisdom and the bab was his mouthpiece.

    The tradition of organising the majalis al-hikma through the bab started at the inception of the Fatimid state and continued until the demise of their dynasty (see Halm, The Fatimids, 23, 79; idem, ‘The Ismaili Oath’, passim). In time, these sessions became formalised for different audiences, with separate ones for Ismaili women. The bab also used the ‘sessions of wisdom’ to administer the oath of allegiance to the Ismaili initiates. There are different compilations of the ‘sessions of wisdom’ of the Fatimid period, which reached their peak in the 800 lectures produced by al-Mu’ayyad fi al-Din al-Shirazi.

    Changes in Ismaili Da‘wa after the Fatimid period

    After the demise of the Fatimids, and following the split of the Ismailis into the Musta‘li-Tayyibi and the Nizari branches, the organisation of the Ismaili da‘wa underwent major change. The position of the bab lost its earlier significance and eventually disappeared altogether. The Musta‘li-Tayyibi Ismailis, who initially managed to establish the stronghold of their da‘wa in Yemen, maintained the organisation of the Fatimid da‘wa and its hierarchy, including the bab, as a conceptual framework as described by the da‘i Hamid al-Din al-Kirmani in his Rahat al-‘aql (see also ‘Masa’il’, 8, 82, 102; ‘Risala’, 175–177).

    The Tayyibi Musta‘li belief of the Imam, his bab and hujjas going into concealment

    The organisation of the Tayyibi da‘wa was first described by the da‘i Hatim b. Ibrahim al-Hamidi (d. 596 AH / 1199 CE) in his Tuhfat al-qulub which still remains unpublished. In comparison to the organisation of the Fatimid da‘wa, it was less elaborate and had fewer ranks in its hierarchy. After the murder of the Fatimid caliph al-Amir bi-Ahkam Allah in 524 AH / 1130 CE, according to the beliefs of the Musta‘li-Tayyibi Ismailis, the imam, his bab and hujjas all went into concealment. Since the time of al-Tayyib, al-Amir’s son and the twenty-first Tayyibi imam, they have all remained in hiding generation after generation until the present time. Starting with al-Dhu’ayb b. Musa al-Wadi‘i (d. 546/1151), the executive head of the Musta‘li-Tayyibi da‘wa has been known as al-da‘i al-mutlaq, or the da‘i with absolute authority.

    Division within the Musta‘li-Tayyibi da‘wa

    Towards the end of the 10th AH / 16th CE century following the division of the Musta‘li-Tayyibi da‘wa and community into its main Da’udi and Sulaymani branches, each of these communities has been led by a separate line of da‘i mutlaqs. For both branches, the da‘i mutlaqs act as the head of the da‘wa and community with full authority over all affairs of the members, with each da‘i appointing his successor. Each da‘i mutlaq, especially in Yemen, has responsibilities similar to the Fatimid bab concerning the Ismaili education of the members of the da‘wa (Daftary, The Isma‘ilis, 275). A third branch of the Tayyibi da‘wa was founded in India after the 28th Da’udi da‘i, by ‘Ali b. Ibrahim (d. 1046 AH/1637 CE); this community, located in India and known as ‘Alawi Bohras, has followed another line of da‘i mutlaqs.

    Nizari Da ‘wa during the Alamut Period

    Information on the organisation of the Nizari Ismaili da‘wa during the Alamut period (483–654 AH / 1090–1256 CE) is scarce. For several decades after Nizar b. al-Mustansir (d. 488 AH / 1095 CE), the Nizari imams remained in hiding and were not accessible to their community. During this period of concealment the leadership of the Nizari da‘wa, state and community was vested in the hands of the imam’s hujja, or chief representative, whose position was equivalent to the bab al-abwab of earlier times (Daftary, The Isma‘ilis, 301–302).
