    BIOGRAPHIES

    Karen Bauer is a Research Associate in the Qur’anic Studies unit at the Institute of Ismaili Studies, London; she previouslyobtained a PhD from Princeton University in 2008. Dr Bauerspecialises in classical Islamic intellectual and social history, with a focus on women’s status in tafsir and the Qur’an. She is interested in the effect of genre, intellectual context and social context on the articulation of Islamic thought. Her publications address diverse topics such as whether women can act as judges according to Islamic law, the audience of classical works of tafsir, and the status of women in the Qur’an.She has organised a number of conferences and forums, and is the editor of a volume on the theories and methods of Qur’anic interpretation entitled The Aims and Methods of Qur’anic Exegesis, 8th–15th Centuries (Oxford University Press in association with the Institute of Ismaili Studies, 2013).

    Alice Bombardier is currently a Research Associate at the Centre d’Analyse et d’Intervention Sociologiques–l’École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales (CADIS–EHESS), Paris, and specialises in modern and contemporary Iranian painting. Her publications on Iranian revolutionary painting, particularly on the murals with anthropomorphic representations devoted to the figure of the martyr located in Khorramshahr’s mosque, include ‘Peinture de guerre et représentations anthropomorphiques dans un lieu de prière musulman en Iran’, Asiatische Studien 3 (2012) and ‘War Painting and Pilgrimage in Iran’, Visual Anthropology, 25/1–2 (2012).

    Nicholas John Boylston is a PhD candidate at Georgetown University in the Theological and Religious Studies Program, which focuses on religious pluralism. He has been a visiting researcher and editor of Englishlanguage publicationsat the Iranian Institute of Philosophy in Tehran and received an MA in Islamic Philosophy from the University of Tehran in 2011. He is currently writing a dissertation on the significance of the religious other in Persian mystical poetry, and is conducting research on interactions between Sufi and Shi‘i intellectualtraditions.

    Rainer Brunner has been Directeur de Recherche at the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS), Paris, since 2005. Before that he was a Research Associate at the Orientalisches Seminar of Freiburg University (1998–2004), Directeur d’Études Invité at the École Pratique des Hautes Études, Section Sciences Religieuses at the Sorbonne (2002), Fellow at the Institute for Advanced Studies of the Hebrew University Jerusalem (2002–2003), and was a member of the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton (2004–2005). His main research interests are modern Muslim intellectual history, especially the history and theology of the Shi‘a, the relationship between the Sunni and the Shi‘a, and Islam in Europe.

    Alessandro Cancian is a Research Associate in the Qur’anic Studies unitat the Institute of Ismaili Studies, London. He completed a PhD at the University of Siena in Anthropology, concentrating on the Cultural Anthropology of Muslim Societies and the Anthropology of Religion. His dissertation was on the Shi‘i theological colleges (hawza ʿilmiyyas) in Syria. He is a review editor for the Journal of Shi‘a Islamic Studies, and has edited and published articles and papers, contributed book chapters and encyclopaedia entries and lectures. Dr Cancian’s areas of interest and expertise are the intellectual history of Shi‘ism, Shi‘i Sufims in early modern times and the anthropology of Islam, Shi‘ism and modern Iran. He is currently working on Shi‘i mystical exegesis of the Qur’an, its influences and reception in modern times, and is writing a monograph on Sultan ʿAli Shah’s Tafsir Bayan al-saʿada.

    Giovanni De Zorzi is a Researcher at the Ca’ Foscari University of Venice. He holds a PhD in Ethnomusicology and is both a lecturer in Ethnomusicology and a musician (ney flute player). His main research areas are the Ottoman, Iranian and Central Asian traditions of classical and Sufi music. He is the author of the recent book Musiche di Turchia. Tradizioni e Transiti tra Oriente ed Occidente (Milan, 2010) as well as the leader of the Ensemble Maraghi (http://www.ensemblemaraghi.it/).

    Ingvild Flaskerud is a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Faculty of Theology, University of Oslo. She holds a PhD in Religious Studies from the University of Bergen.Based on ethnographic research, her publications focus on various aspects of Twelver Shi‘ism in its everyday practice. Dr Flaskerud’s research on Iranian Shi‘ism has resulted in the production of an ethnographic film, and several articles and book chapters on Iranian Shi‘i women’s rituals. She is also interested in the visual and material aspects of religion, hence her publication of Visualizing Belief and Piety in Iranian Shiism (London and New York, 2010). She has worked on the transfer of religion (with a particular focus on Shi‘i Islam) and cultural formation in Western migrancy as part of the international research project ‘Muslim Devotional Practices: Aesthetics and Cultural Formation in Migrancy’ funded by the Norwegian Research Council, and has joined the international research project ‘Negotiating Authority in Contemporary Shi‘ite Thought and Practice’ funded by the Swedish Research Council..

    Seyyed Mohammad Hadi Gerami is a Visiting Lecturer at Imam Sadiq University, Tehran. He has published a number of monographs and articles on the history of Shiʻism, the history of hadith and tafsir, the social analysis of the early Imāmī communities and anti-rationalism movements in the Islamic world, among other things.

    Omid Ghaemmaghami earned a PhD in Middle Eastern and Islamic Studies from the University of Toronto (2013). He has taught courses in Islamic Studies as well as introductory and advanced Arabic at the University of Toronto and Middlebury College, and is currently pursuing a second MA in Teaching Arabic as a Foreign Language (TAFL) at the American University in Cairo. His research interests are Qur’anic exegesis, Shi‘i Islam in its formative period and Islamic eschatology. He has published several articles and translations in these and other areas, and is currently preparing the forthcoming monograph,‘Seeing the Proof: The Question of Contacting the Hidden Imam in Early Twelver Shi‘i Islam’.

    Niloofar Haeri is Professor and Chair of the Department of Anthropology at Johns Hopkins University. Her earlier work dealt with the issue of language, gender and class in Egypt, and later, her major project examined the layered ramifications of the ambiguity of classical Arabic as the sacred language of the Qur’an but also as the official state language of Egypt. The examination of that ambiguity also entailed a historical analysis of the rejection of vernacular Arabic as the language of writing. Her present work on namaz/salat is based on ongoing fieldwork in Tehran that began a few years ago. Sparked by an interest in exploring the varying experiences that different reciters have, the work has already resulted in an article on repetition, time and meaning in the private performance of namaz.

    Denis Hermann is a Senior Research Fellow at the Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique (CNRS), Paris. He is a specialist in the modern and contemporary periods of Iran, and is particularly interested in the intellectual and social history of Shi‘ism in the Qājār period and the constitutional movement. He is the author of several articles, including ‘Akhund Khurasani and the Constitutional Movement’, Middle Eastern Studies (2013). He has also edited two volumes: (with Sabrina Mervin) Shi‘i Trends and Dynamics in Modern Times (XVIIIrd–XXth centuries)/ Courants et dynamiques chiites à l’époque moderne (XVIIIe–XXe siècles) (Beirut and Würzburg, 2010)and (with Fabrizio Speziale) Muslim Cultures in the Indo-Iranian World during the Early-Modern and Modern Periods (Tehran and Berlin, 2010).

    Seyfeddin Kara is currently a PhD Student, and a tutor for the course Introduction to Islam and the Muslim World, at the School of Government and International Affairs, Durham University. His PhD topic is the early Shi‘ite narrations on the history of the collection of the Qur’an. Before embarking on his PhD, Seyfeddin worked as a researcher in the field of human rights for five years.

    Mirjam Künkler is Assistant Professor in the Department for Near Eastern Studies at Princeton University. She holds a PhD from Columbia University, New York. Her research interests concern religion-state relations and Islamic thought in 20th century Iran and Indonesia. Her recent publications include (co-edited with Alfred Stepan)Indonesia, Islam and Democracy (New York,2013), “The Special Court of the Clergy (Dadgah-e Vizheh-ye Ruhaniyat) and the Repression of Dissident Clergy in Iran” in Said Arjomand and Nathan Brown (eds.), Constitutionalism, the Rule of Law and the Politics of Administration in Egypt and Iran (Albany, NY, 2013), as well as two special issues she guest edited for the Journal of Law and Religion and the Journal Party Politics. In her new project, she turns to questions around female religious authority in Twelver Shi‘ism. Künkler is co-Principal Investigator of the Princeton-Oxford project on “Traditional authority and transnational religious networks in contemporary Shi‘ism”, and of the Iran Social Science Data Portal funded by the Social Science Research Council(SSRC).

    Leonard Lewisohn is a Senior Lecturer in Persian and Iran Heritage Foundation Fellow in Classical Persian and Sufi Literature at the Institute of Arab and Islamic Studies of the University of Exeter, where he currently teaches Persian language, Sufism, the history of Iran, as well as courses on Persian texts and Persian poetry in translation. He specialises in the translation of Persian Sufi poetry and prose. He is the author of Beyond Faith and Infidelity: The Sufi Poetry and Teachings of Mahmud Shabistari (London, 1995) and the editor of three volumes on The Heritage of Sufism (Oxford, 1999). He is editor of the Mawlana Rumi Review, an annual journal devoted to Jalal al-Din Rumi. He is also editor (with Christopher Shackle) of The Art of Spiritual Flight: Farid al-Din ‘Aṭṭaar and the Persian Sufi Tradition (London, 2006), co-translator with Robert Bly of The Angels Knocking on the Tavern Door: Thirty Poems of Hafiz (New York, 2008), and editor of Hafiz and the Religion of Love in Classical Persian Poetry (London, 2010). Dr Lewisohn has contributed articles to several encyclopaedias and journals.

    Mohammad Mesbahi is the Head of Education at the Islamic College, London., in collaboration with the Middlesex University. He gained a PhD in Medical Tomography, in which field he has a number of academic papers and publications. One of his main research focuses is Islamic education in the West and its complexities. Based on his interest in the teaching of Islam in mainstream education in the UK and the importance of curriculum development to Muslims, he has taken part in the development of undergraduate and postgraduate degrees and diplomas at the Islamic College under the Middlesex University collaborative scheme. He is currently in charge of developing a Doctorate programme on Muslim Cultures and Civilisation at the Islamic College

    Yaser Mirdamadi is currently a PhD candidate in Islamic Studies at the University of Edinburgh and is the current recipient of the IIS Mohammad Arkoun Doctoral Scholarship. He spent eight years in an Islamic seminary. His main areas of research are modern Islam and the philosophy of religion.

    Nacim Pak-Shiraz is a Lecturer in Persian and Film Studies at the University of Edinburgh and is currently the Director for the new MSc in Persian Civilisation. She obtained her PhD in Film and Media Studies from the University of London’s School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS), where she studied the engagement between film and religion, with a focus on Shi‘i Islam and Iranian cinema. In 2002, she directed and produced an ethnographic documentary, Dream of Flight, on the lives of Iranian refugees in Van, Turkey. She joined the University of Edinburgh in 2010 after having taught at the Institute of Ismaili Studies between 2007 and 2010. . She is the author of Shiʿi Islam in Iranian Cinema: Religion and Spirituality in Film (London, 2011).

    Sajjad Rizvi is an Associate Professor of Islamic Intellectual History at the University of Exeter. Trained as a historian at Oxford and then Cambridge, he specialises in Islamic thought in the post-classical period and has published on the philosophers of the Safavid and Qajar periods in particular. His other main interest lies in exegesis and Qur’anic hermeneutics. He is the co-editor with Feras Hamza (and Farhana Mayer) of An Anthology of Qurʾanic Commentaries Volume One: The Nature of God (Oxford, 2009). He is currently writing a number of studies on ʿAllama Tabatabaʾi, investigating the nature of hermeneutics and metaphysics in contemporary Islamic thought in the Twelver Shi‘i context.

    Reza Tabandeh has recently gained a PhD in Islamic Studies from the Institute of Arab and Islamic Studies in the University of Exeter, with a specialisation in the revival of Niʿmatullahi Sufism in Iran. His research interest lies in Islamic mystical philosophies in contemporary Iran. His doctoral work focused on the second generation of Niʿmatullahi masters, during the period following the return of the order to Iran from India.

    Liyakat Takim is the Sharjah Chair in Global Islam at McMaster University, Canada. A native of Zanzibar, Tanzania, he has spoken at more than eighty academic conferences and authored 100 scholarly works on diverse topics such as the reformation in the Islamic world, the treatment of women in Islamic law, Islam in America, the indigenisation of the Muslim community in America, dialogue in post-911 America, war and peace in the Islamic tradition, Islamic law, Islamic biographical literature, the charisma of the holy man and shrine culture, and Islamic mystical traditions. He teaches a wide range of courses on Islam and offers a course on comparative religions. Professor Takim’s publications include Shi‘ism in America (New York, 2009) and The Heirs of the Prophet: Charisma and Religious Authority in Shi‘ite Islam (Albany, NY, 2006). He is working on his third book, ‘Ijtihad and Reformation in Islam’. Professor Takim has taught at several American and Canadian universities and is actively engaged in dialogue with different faith communities.

    Anna Vanzan teaches Islamic and Arabic Culture at the ‘Statale’ University in Milan, and is a Visiting Lecturer of Gender and Islamic Thought at the European Masters in International Management (MIM) at Ca’ Foscari University of Venice. She holds a PhD in Near Eastern Studies from New York University. She works on gender studies with a particular focus on Asian Muslim societies. Her latest book, Le donne di Allah, viaggio nei femminismi islamici (Milan, 2010) is dedicated to the emergence of Islamic feminisms, especially in Iran, Turkey, the Balkans, Malaysia and in the Western diaspora.

    Neguin Yavari is a Faculty Member of the Committee on Historical Studies at the New School for Social Research in New York and specialises in topics related to the Middle East. She is currently conducting research on comparative intellectual history, specifically on the mirrors for princes. Her latest article on contemporary Qur’anic hermeneutics,‘Tafsir and The Mythology of Islamic Fundamentalism’, is forthcoming in the volumeTafsir and Islamic Intellectual History, edited by Andreas Görke and Johanna Pink (Oxford University Press, in association with The Institute of Ismaili Studies, 2013).
