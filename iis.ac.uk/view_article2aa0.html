    IIS Alumni explore Secularisation and Religion in the Modern World
    March 2014

    The European Chapter Group (ECG) of the IIS Alumni Association met for its annual meeting in Rome, Italy, in October 2013. The meeting began with opening remarks from the then Chapter President, Dr Otambek Mastibekov, (GPISH 2002), who introduced the theme Secularisation and Religion in the Modern World and the objectives of the meeting.Recalling his first class with the late Professor Mohammed Arkoun, he stated, ‘In his first lesson Prof. Mohammed Arkoun advised us to buy The New Fontana Dictionary of Modern Thought. When the term secularisation was looked up in that dictionary, the first sentence referred to secularisation as the decline of religion.’ Dr Otambek explained that this anecdote could help to put into perspective the challenges between secularisation and religion in the modern world.

    Prof. Paolo Luigi Branca, Facoltà di Lettere e Filosofia, Sede di Milano, Dipartimento di Scienze religiose, (Faculty of Arts and Philosophy, Milan Campus, Department of Religious Studies), presented on Religion and Secularism: Between Opposition and Complementarity. Referring to Islam, Prof. Branca noted that when religion became an ideology it faced the same fate as other ideologies. He suggested that religion should never be an unchanging ideology because the changing reality of the world is part of God’s creation. Prof. Branca described how, with the evolution of science, God was no longer at the centre of society and the rise of the middle class started to play a role in the social dynamics of society. Teachings of religion, he said, became more repetitive than creative. However, in recent times, religion has gained a new role in the public space because of the crisis of secularism.

    Prof. Alberto Melloni, from Istituto dela Enciclopedia Italiana – Treccani, (Institute of the Italian Encyclopaedia), spoke on Vatican II and Otherness. He highlighted the fact that, since the Middle Ages, one of the defining points of the Christianitas was its difference in comparison to other religions. Prof. Melloni described how modernity challenged this idea from the outside: trying to reach and to impose on the Churches a neutral space of citizenship and rights where ecclesiastical laws and condemnations had to be ineffective. The result of this effort from the 19th till the 20th centuries is what is now called Western constitutionalism, which emerged from this separation of church and state under a principle of laicité.

    The second half of the meeting included a presentation from Dr Otared Haidar, (GPISH 1998), currently with University of Oxford, on The Syrian Ismailis and Salamiyah: A Lost Chapter in the Historiography of the Arab Renaissance. She discussed the established concepts of modernity, enlightenment and renaissance in the major reference and encyclopaedic works that inform the teaching, research and scholarship in both Western and Arab academia in order to bring about a better understanding of crucial gaps in the historiography of this crucial period. Her presentation is part of a larger body of research that explores some overlooked areas of the movement of Arab enlightenment and modernity in Syria and Lebanon. The part of the research she presented, examined how the contribution of the Syrian Ismailis to Arab enlightenment movement was based strongly on two models: the Fatimid tradition of learning and modern education, and the combination of both continuity and change.

    Mr. Steve Lewitt, the Head of Human Resources at the IIS, conveyed to the alumni their continuing importance as a resource for the IIS. He shared with the alumni his knowledge and experience of recruitment at the IIS and encouraged them to consider careers with the IIS. The day concluded with a presentation from Mr. Shiraz Kabani,Head of Community Relations at the IIS. He highlighted the importance of the alumni body being fully aware of the Institute’s work and the rationale behind its projects and publications, in order to enhance the awareness and impact of the work and also to complement their own intellectual development. Given the growing number of STEP alumni, Mr Kabani provided an insight into the evolution of the STEP programme and the Institute’s vision for the future.

    On the second day, Dr Dagikhudo Dagiev (GPISH 2003), Research Associate at the IIS, spoke about Secularisation and Religion in Contemporary Central Asia. In particular, he discussed how the re-emergence of Islam in post-Soviet Central Asia coincided with the ideological vacuum that came about with the decline of Communism. The ex-Communist leaders’ fear, explained Dr Dagiev, was that Islam as a religio-political ideology could challenge the legitimacy of their rule as well as the secular form of government. However, secularism is not about separation of the state from religion; rather, it is about the protection of religious freedoms and ensuring the freedoms of thought and conscience equally to all believers and non-believers. In the context of the post-Communist Central Asian states, secularism is understood differently, as religion has largely been absent from public life.

    The highlight of the meeting was a Keynote Address by IIS Governor Dr Aziz Esmail in which he shared further reflections on the subject of Religion and Secularism.
